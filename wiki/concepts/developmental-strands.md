# Developmental Strands

A framework for understanding human development as progress along multiple simultaneous tracks, each representing a distinct domain of psychological growth. Originally proposed by Anna Freud (1965); adapted by [[lisa-damour|Lisa Damour]] for [[teenage-girl-development|teenage girl development]].

## Origin

Anna Freud (daughter of Sigmund Freud, and a major psychoanalyst in her own right) proposed developmental strands in 1965 as a way to organize the normal turmoil of childhood development. She argued that children advance simultaneously on multiple fronts — from dependency to self-reliance, from play to work, from egocentricity to companionship — and that healthy development can be assessed by progress along these strands, not by any single metric.

## Key Properties

- **Simultaneous progression** — development happens on all strands at once; a child may be advanced on one and delayed on another without this being pathological
- **Non-linear** — progress is uneven; temporary regressions on a strand are normal, especially under stress
- **Domain-specific assessment** — to evaluate health, you ask "how is this person progressing on each strand?" not "are they normal overall?"
- **Normal range** — each strand has a wide normal range; the framework helps distinguish expected variation from genuine disorder

## Damour's Seven Strands for Teenage Girls

1. Parting with childhood
2. Joining a new tribe (peer groups)
3. Harnessing emotions
4. Contending with adult authority
5. Planning for the future
6. Entering the romantic world
7. Caring for herself

See [[teenage-girl-development]] for detailed descriptions of each strand.

## Clinical Application

The strands framework helps clinicians and parents:
- Normalize confusing behavior by locating it within a developmental trajectory
- Identify genuine stalls or regressions that warrant intervention
- Avoid being misled by success in one domain into overlooking difficulty in another
