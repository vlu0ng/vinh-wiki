# Mental Models

Simplified representations of how the world works, drawn from different disciplines, used as cognitive tools for reasoning and decision-making. The concept is most associated with [[charlie-munger|Charlie Munger]], who built a lifelong "latticework" of models from physics, biology, psychology, history, economics, and mathematics.

## Key Properties

- **Cross-disciplinary** — the most useful mental models generalize across domains; a model from thermodynamics (entropy) can illuminate organizational decay; a model from biology (ecological niches) can illuminate competitive strategy
- **Complementary** — no single model is sufficient; holding multiple models reduces the distortion of viewing every problem through one lens
- **Cognitive shortcuts** — models allow faster pattern recognition by encoding prior learning; the risk is applying the wrong model to a situation
- **Improvable** — models should be tested against reality and updated when they fail; dogmatic attachment to a model is itself a cognitive error

## Key Mental Models (Munger's Canon)

| Model | Source | Application |
|---|---|---|
| [[inversion|Inversion]] | Mathematics (Jacobi) | Reason backward from failure to identify what to avoid |
| Circle of Competence | Investing | Know your knowledge boundaries; stay within them |
| Opportunity Cost | Economics | Every choice forecloses alternatives; price everything |
| Incentive-Caused Bias | Psychology | Behavior reliably follows incentives; ask "who benefits?" |
| Lollapalooza Effect | Psychology | Multiple aligned forces produce non-linear extremes |
| Margin of Safety | Engineering/Investing | Build in buffers against your own errors and surprise |
| Mean Reversion | Statistics | Extreme values tend to regress toward averages over time |

## The Latticework Metaphor

Munger describes well-functioning models as a "latticework" — a structure where models reinforce and cross-reference each other. Knowledge built into a latticework is available for recall when needed and resists forgetting because it is richly interconnected.

## Relationship to Behavioral Economics

Munger's "Psychology of Human Misjudgment" (cataloging 25+ cognitive biases) was developed largely independently of academic behavioral economics (Kahneman, Tversky). The two converge substantially, though Munger's approach is more eclectic and less statistically rigorous.
