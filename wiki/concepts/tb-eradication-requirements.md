# TB Eradication — Political and Financial Requirements

What it would actually take, politically and financially, to drive global TB deaths to near-zero — synthesized from John Green's *Everything Is Tuberculosis* and the [[partners-in-health|Partners In Health]] case record.

## Why TB Is Politically Tractable

TB is the rare global-health problem where the technical question is essentially settled:

- TB has been **curable since 1952**.
- The first-line drug course (RIPE — rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) was cheap by the 1990s.
- The 2023 endTB trial showed that even **multi-drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB)** is now ~90% curable at **about $300 per course** — a 98% reduction from the $15,000–$20,000 cost in the late 1990s.
- The Partners In Health Peru program in the late 1990s achieved >85% MDR-TB cure rates in low-resource settings, demonstrating that the bottleneck is delivery, not pharmacology.

The remaining barriers are political and financial. They fall into four categories.

## 1. Health-System Infrastructure (Largest Cost)

Drugs without a delivery system don't cure people. The chronic constraint is human infrastructure:

- **Trained health workers** — diagnosticians, clinicians, and especially **community health workers (CHWs)** who live in affected communities and accompany patients through 6-month (drug-sensitive) to 2-year+ (drug-resistant) treatment courses. The PIH "accompaniment" model is the operational template: case detection, adherence support, nutrition support, and side-effect management all delivered at the household level.
- **Diagnostic capacity** — sputum microscopy misses ~50% of cases (Phumeza Tisile's case is the canonical example: she was told she was negative twice before an X-ray showed both lungs choked with TB). Replacing microscopy with molecular diagnostics (GeneXpert) is feasible but requires equipment, supply chains, and trained operators in every district hospital and many primary-care clinics.
- **Cold chains, sample transport, and labs** for drug-susceptibility testing.

The WHO Stop TB Partnership Global Plan to End TB (2023–2030) estimates the financing gap at roughly **$22 billion per year** globally, with **$5 billion per year for TB R&D** as a separate line. Current global spending is roughly half the operational figure. The implication: full eradication needs roughly a **doubling of present TB-specific spending sustained for at least a decade**, with most new dollars going to delivery infrastructure rather than drug procurement.

This figure is small relative to global defense spending (~$2.4 trillion annually) and modest even relative to global health aid as a whole. It is large relative to the historical willingness of donor governments to fund TB specifically.

## 2. Drug Pricing and Patent Regimes

Even after the price collapse, drug pricing remains a structural lever:

- **Bedaquiline** (Johnson & Johnson, first released 2013) cost $900 per course in low-income countries and $3,000 in middle-income countries. J&J's primary patent was set to expire in 2023; the company attempted **patent evergreening** — filing secondary patents on an adjuvant compound — to extend protection. TB survivors Phumeza Tisile and Nandita Venkatesan filed a patent challenge in Indian courts and won; J&J subsequently backed down on enforcing secondary patents in most low- and middle-income countries, and the price dropped over 60% almost overnight.
- **Pretomanid** (TB Alliance, 2019) is the other newer MDR-TB drug; its pricing has been more accessible because it was developed by a non-profit but is still subject to country-by-country negotiation.
- **Generic manufacturing** is the most reliable price-reduction mechanism. Indian and South African generic manufacturers were essential to the 1990s–2000s price collapse on first-line drugs and on antiretrovirals; the same model works for newer TB drugs only if patents do not block them.

The political requirement: **TRIPS flexibilities (compulsory licensing) consistently exercised by middle-income countries**, sustained pressure on pharmaceutical firms to license to generics in low-income markets, and the political insulation to do this in the face of US Trade Representative pushback. Carole Mitnick's framing — *"cost-effectiveness is a moving target"* — captures the dynamic: prices that were "impossible" in 1998 became routine by 2023 specifically because PIH and others refused the cost-effectiveness frame and treated it instead as a pricing problem.

## 3. The "Cost-Effectiveness" Narrative

The single most stubborn political obstacle Green identifies is the **cost-effectiveness logic** that historically deprioritized TB in low-income settings:

- WHO's 1990s guidance that **"MDR-TB is too expensive to treat in poor countries."**
- The standard of care for MDR-TB was "supportive therapy" — Dr. Carole Mitnick's gloss: *"Put sick people in a hut on the side of the road and wait for them to die."*
- Paul Farmer's counter-argument: *"failure to diagnose and treat MDR-TB is what is really costly"* — because untreated MDR-TB spreads and mutates further.

The narrative requirement for eradication is the displacement of static cost-effectiveness with **dynamic, system-level accounting**: the cost of treating today vs. the cost of resistance, ongoing transmission, lost productivity, and orphaned children tomorrow. The COVID parallel — global vaccine access as self-interest, not charity (see [[pandemic-lessons-from-tb]]) — is the same argument applied to a different disease.

Political eradication requires shifting decision-makers from "cost per QALY in country X" to "cost of letting a curable pathogen circulate globally." This is more a framing problem than an arithmetic problem.

## 4. Sustained Funding Across Political Cycles

TB programs are not one-shot interventions; the disease is durable. The political requirement most often missed:

- **Multi-decade funding commitments** through institutions insulated from single-administration policy reversals. The Global Fund, USAID PEPFAR-style mechanisms, and the WHO are the existing scaffolding; none currently has guaranteed TB funding at the scale required.
- **Country co-financing trajectories** so that high-burden middle-income countries (India, South Africa, the Philippines, Brazil, Indonesia) take over progressively larger shares as their economies grow. India accounts for ~28% of global TB burden; its domestic TB budget has grown but remains below need.
- **Resilience to political shocks** — funding pauses (e.g., from US foreign-aid reviews) directly translate into interrupted treatment, which is the exact mechanism that produces drug resistance.

The historical pattern Green emphasizes: TB has been the world's deadliest infectious disease through most of recorded history; **the political will required is not new spending so much as new continuity of spending.**

## What "Eradication" Means Here

True biological eradication of TB — like smallpox — is currently not on the table. *M. tuberculosis* has no animal reservoir but ~25% of the world's population carries latent infection, of whom ~10% develop active disease over a lifetime. Without an effective vaccine (BCG is partial and primarily protects against childhood TB meningitis), eliminating the latent reservoir is multi-generational.

What is achievable on a 10–25 year horizon, given the political and financial inputs above:

- **Elimination as a public-health problem** (WHO definition: <10 cases per million population) in most countries
- **Reduction of global deaths from ~1.25 million per year to <100,000**
- **Containment of MDR-TB transmission** through universal access to drug-susceptibility testing and full second-line treatment

The WHO "End TB" 2030 targets (90% reduction in deaths and 80% reduction in incidence vs. 2015) are this kind of "eradication-as-elimination" goal. They are off-track as of the mid-2020s, but the shortfall is funding and political continuity, not technology.

## The Summary Equation

Full elimination of TB as a public-health problem requires, in rough terms:

- **~$22B/year** in delivery and treatment funding, sustained for ~15 years
- **~$5B/year** in R&D for shorter-course drugs and an effective adult vaccine
- **Patent regime that does not block generic production** of new TB drugs in high-burden countries
- **Health-system infrastructure** (CHWs, diagnostics, labs) built primarily in low- and middle-income countries
- **Narrative shift** away from static cost-effectiveness toward dynamic transmission accounting
- **Multi-decade political continuity** across donor and recipient governments

The remarkable feature of TB is that none of these requirements is unknown or technically novel. As John Green's central argument has it: *the problem is moral and political, not scientific.* That diagnosis also points at the cure.
