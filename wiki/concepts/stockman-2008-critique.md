# Stockman's 2008 Critique: Was Intervention Necessary?

David Stockman's central empirical claim in *The Great Deformation*: the 2008 TARP bailout was unnecessary — a "Blackberry Panic" manufactured by Goldman Sachs alumni to extract public subsidies. This claim does not hold up against the empirical record, though Stockman's moral critique of *how* the intervention was executed has substantial validity.

## What Stockman Claims

Stockman calls the 2008 crisis response a "hissy fit on Wall Street" driven by panic rather than systemic necessity. His argument: there were no dark ATMs, no imminent collapse of the real economy — just Wall Street melodrama that Hank Paulson (a Goldman transplant) exploited to direct massive public transfers to financial firms. The "contagion" narrative was a cover story for crony capitalism in action.

## What the Empirical Record Shows

The measurable disruptions in September-October 2008 are incompatible with the "manufactured crisis" framing:

- **Commercial paper markets froze**: Short-term credit that corporations depend on for payroll and operations stopped functioning as investors fled risk.
- **Money market funds broke the buck**: Immediately after Lehman's collapse, the Reserve Primary Fund fell below $1/share, triggering a run on the $3.5 trillion money-market industry. The government had to explicitly guarantee money market funds to stop the run.
- **Interbank lending essentially halted**: LIBOR spreads spiked to levels indicating banks would not lend to each other — the core mechanism by which credit flows through the economy.

Ben Bernanke — a serious scholar of the Great Depression, not a Wall Street insider — was specifically responding to the mechanism by which 1929 became 1932: bank-run contagion propagating through the real economy via credit contraction. His response was shaped by the lesson that insufficient intervention in 1930-31 converted a recession into a catastrophe.

Counterfactual analysis (Gorton; Acemoglu and others) suggests that without intervention, cascading institutional failures could have pushed unemployment well above the ~10% actual peak — potentially toward Depression-era levels.

## Where Stockman's Libertarian Priors Mislead Him

Stockman reads the presence of **moral hazard** (which is real and severe) as proof that the underlying systemic risk was manufactured. But both can be true simultaneously:

- Systemic risk was genuine → intervention of some kind was probably necessary to prevent a depression
- The specific intervention was structured to benefit financial firms at public expense → moral hazard was created and not addressed

His libertarian framework has difficulty holding both propositions at once. The existence of crony capture in the bailout design does not retroactively eliminate the systemic risk that made some intervention necessary.

## Where Stockman Is Correct

His critique of *how* the intervention was executed has substantial merit:

1. **Bailout terms were too favorable to shareholders and executives**: Goldman Sachs received 100 cents on the dollar on AIG counterparty contracts; equity shareholders who bore the risk were not wiped out as would occur in a normal bankruptcy
2. **"Too big to fail" was left structurally unaddressed**: The post-crisis period locked in the very dynamic Stockman criticizes — banks emerged larger, more concentrated, and with implicit government backing more entrenched than before
3. **Subsequent QE went far beyond crisis-fighting**: Once the acute phase passed, the Fed continued asset-purchase programs that concentrated gains to capital owners and suppressed the market-clearing processes that normal recessions accomplish
4. **Moral hazard was severe**: Firms that made catastrophic bets were rescued with limited consequences for decision-makers, setting incentives for future risk-taking

## The Correct Synthesis

Intervention of *some* kind was probably necessary to prevent a depression. The specific intervention that occurred was poorly structured — captured by the interests it was meant to discipline — and generated severe long-term moral hazard. Stockman is the right diagnostician of the pathology and the wrong analyst of whether surgery was required.
