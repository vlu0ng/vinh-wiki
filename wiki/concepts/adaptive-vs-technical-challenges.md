# Adaptive vs. Technical Challenges

The core diagnostic distinction in Ronald Heifetz's Adaptive Leadership framework. Most hard organizational problems
fail not because of incompetence but because people apply *technical* solutions to *adaptive* challenges.

## Definitions

**Technical problem**: A problem where the solution is known, or knowable by an expert. The problem can be defined
clearly; the authority (manager, doctor, engineer) can solve it without requiring those affected to fundamentally
change.

**Adaptive challenge**: A problem that requires people to *change their values, beliefs, attitudes, or behaviors*
to solve. No authority can do this work for them. The "solution" is not a policy or a process — it is learning and
change in the people who face the challenge.

## Examples

| Technical | Adaptive |
|---|---|
| Fix a software bug | Change engineering team culture to prevent recurring bugs |
| Treat a heart attack | Get a patient to change diet/exercise habits permanently |
| Increase employee benefits | Address disengagement caused by meaningless work |
| Install new ERP software | Shift organization from siloed to collaborative processes |

Most real-world challenges are *mixed*: they have technical components that experts can solve and adaptive components
that require human change.

## Why the Distinction Matters

Leaders repeatedly fail by:
1. Treating adaptive challenges as technical (hiring consultants, deploying processes) → the "fix" doesn't stick
   because the underlying human resistance is untouched
2. Solving technical problems for people who need to develop the capability themselves → creates dependency, not capacity

## Application: Diagnosis First

Heifetz's recommended sequence: *diagnose before prescribing*. Ask:
- Is there a known solution? → Technical
- Does solving this require people to change? → Adaptive
- Does it require people to give up something they value? → Definitely adaptive
