# Intellectual Property and Health Equity

The structural relationship between intellectual property (IP) laws, patent protections, and global disparities in access to life-saving healthcare. This concept examines how international trade agreements and patent regimes construct artificial scarcity, driving up drug prices and concentrating health innovations in wealthy markets while creating systemic neglect in low-income populations.

## The Mechanism of Artificial Scarcity

Modern drug development relies heavily on the patent system to incentivize research and development (R&D). A patent grants a pharmaceutical corporation a legal monopoly (typically for 20 years) to manufacture and sell a newly discovered drug, allowing them to charge prices far above the marginal cost of production. 

While this system aims to recoup R&D costs, it creates severe conflicts with [[health-equity]]:
1. **The Allocation of Innovation**: Because profitability depends on purchasing power, pharmaceutical R&D is heavily skewed toward diseases that affect affluent populations in high-income countries (e.g., lifestyle diseases, chronic conditions of aging). Neglected tropical diseases and conditions concentrated in poverty (like tuberculosis) receive disproportionately little investment.
2. **The Distribution Gap**: When a cure is developed, the patent monopoly keeps prices high, effectively pricing out patients in low- and middle-income countries. Dr. Peter Mugyenyi's famous formulation regarding HIV treatment in the 1990s and 2000s applies broadly: *"The drugs are where the disease is not. The disease is where the drugs are not."*
3. **The TRIPS Agreement**: The World Trade Organization's (WTO) Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS) globalized strict patent enforcement in 1995. Although TRIPS contains "flexibilities" (such as compulsory licensing, which allows a country to manufacture generics in a health emergency), high-income countries and pharmaceutical lobbies have historically used intense political and economic pressure to deter developing countries from exercising these rights.

## Case Studies of IP-Driven Inequity

### 1. Tuberculosis (TB)
As highlighted by [[john-green|John Green]] in *Everything Is Tuberculosis*, TB has been curable since the discovery of streptomycin in 1943 and the development of combination regimens in 1952. Yet, 1.25 million people died of this curable disease in 2023. A major factor is the high cost of newer, safer treatments for drug-resistant TB (such as Bedaquiline). For years, patent holder Johnson & Johnson maintained high prices and restricted generic manufacturing, locking out millions in high-burden countries until grassroots global activism forced concessionary licensing.

### 2. HIV/AIDS (The 1990s Crisis)
Highly Active Antiretroviral Therapy (HAART) was introduced in 1996, transforming HIV from a death sentence to a manageable condition in wealthy nations. However, because HAART cost $10,000–$15,000 per patient per year under patent, it was unavailable in Sub-Saharan Africa, where millions died unnecessarily. It was only when generic manufacturers in India (such as Cipla), operating outside WTO constraints at the time, bypassed patents to offer the same regimen for $350 a year (and eventually less than $100) that global treatment could scale through organizations like [[partners-in-health|Partners in Health]].

### 3. COVID-19 Vaccine Nationalism
During the COVID-19 pandemic, mRNA vaccine technologies developed with massive public funding were locked behind proprietary patent barriers. Wealthy nations hoarded doses, while South Africa and India's proposal for a temporary TRIPS patent waiver was blocked by high-income countries protective of their domestic pharmaceutical industries. This delayed global vaccination coverage by years, costing millions of lives and allowing new variants to emerge.

## Alternative Models for Equitable Innovation

To decouple medical research from artificial scarcity, economists and public health advocates have proposed several reforms:
- **Patent Pools**: Voluntary licensing agreements where developers share their IP with a central pool (like the Medicines Patent Pool), allowing generic manufacturers to produce cheap versions for low-income countries in exchange for low royalties.
- **Push and Pull Funding**: Governments and philanthropists directly funding early-stage R&D (push) and pre-committing to buy successful drugs at low prices (pull), eliminating the need for high patent-protected monopoly pricing.
- **The Health Impact Fund**: A proposed international agency that would reward pharmaceutical developers based on the actual health outcomes (disability-adjusted life years saved) their drugs produce globally, provided the drug is distributed at cost. This makes curing diseases among the poorest populations highly profitable.

Decoupling the financing of medical R&D from the price of the final product is a prerequisite for achieving global health equity. Without structural reform to intellectual property law, the fruits of human scientific genius will continue to be distributed along lines of wealth rather than human need.
