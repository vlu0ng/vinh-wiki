# Tiny Experiments

Small, bounded, curiosity-driven trials that prioritize learning over predetermined outcomes — proposed by Anne-Laure Le Cunff as an alternative to linear goal-setting.

## Definition

A tiny experiment is a structured attempt to learn something by doing it in a limited, low-stakes way. Unlike a goal (which defines success as arriving at a target), a tiny experiment defines success as learning something useful — regardless of whether the outcome matches the hypothesis.

## Structure

A well-formed tiny experiment has:
1. **A clear hypothesis** — what do I expect or want to learn?
2. **A bounded commitment** — a specific time window or output count (e.g., "write 100 articles in 100 workdays")
3. **A learning criterion** — what will I observe and what will it tell me?
4. **Permission to iterate** — if the experiment reveals new information, adjust the next experiment accordingly

## Key Properties

- **Small scope** — designed to be completable without major upfront commitment; reduces the cost of failure
- **Curiosity-driven** — motivated by genuine interest in the outcome, not by external pressure or social signaling
- **Fast feedback** — produces observable results quickly enough to inform the next iteration
- **Fail-forward** — failures are valuable data, not defeats; the experimental frame normalizes negative outcomes

## Neuroscience Basis

Le Cunff grounds the concept in research on intrinsic motivation and the brain's reward system. Curiosity-driven behavior (exploring, discovering) activates dopaminergic reward circuits differently from threat-avoidance behavior (avoiding failure, meeting external targets). Experiments sustain intrinsic motivation better than outcome-based goals.

## Examples
- Le Cunff's own "100 articles in 100 workdays" pact → became Ness Labs
- Amelia Earhart's early aviation experiments (passenger flight, photography, trucking) → enabled her solo transatlantic flight
