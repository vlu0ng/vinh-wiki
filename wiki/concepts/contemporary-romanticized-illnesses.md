# Contemporary Romanticized Illnesses

Which present-day diseases carry framings analogous to TB's "artist's disease" myth — illnesses that have been aestheticized, mythologized, or attached to desirable identity traits in ways that obscure the underlying pathology and redistribute political sympathy. Extends the [[romanticization-of-illness]] analysis from the 19th-century case to the 21st-century landscape.

## The Pattern to Look For

Drawing on the structural test from [[romanticization-of-illness]] and [[illness-narrative-and-coping]], a contemporary illness is "romanticized" when it exhibits some combination of:

1. **Symptoms recoded as desirable traits** (pallor → spiritual purity in TB; insomnia → drive; thinness → discipline)
2. **The disease attached to a flattering identity** (artist's disease; high performer's affliction; tortured genius)
3. **Sympathy concentrated on a subset of sufferers** (typically the educated and economically secure) while others bearing the same diagnosis are stigmatized or ignored
4. **Treatment access shaped by the romantic framing** — care infrastructure clustered around the "deserving" subset
5. **Cultural production reinforcing the loop** — memoirs, films, viral content, fashion aesthetics that recycle the romance

## The Major Contemporary Cases

### Depression and Anxiety as "Creative Sensitivity"

The most widely diffused contemporary romanticization is the linkage between mood and anxiety disorders and creative or perceptual gifts. Manifestations:

- The "tortured artist" trope mapped to clinical depression — writers, musicians, comedians.
- The "highly sensitive person" framing that recodes anxiety vulnerability as superior perception.
- The "anxious overachiever" identity that flatters anxiety as the price of high performance.

**Sontag test:** Causal assignment is partly inverted (illness is not caused by moral failing; it is the price of insight). But sympathy concentrates on the productive and articulate, while severe clinical depression — catatonia, treatment-resistant cases, depression in the unhoused — receives no aesthetic protection. The romantic framing makes mild-to-moderate depression *easier* to talk about and, paradoxically, the worst cases harder to see.

This is the question explicitly flagged in [[romanticization-of-illness]]. The [[illness-narrative-and-coping]] resolution applies: community-building uses of the narrative are not necessarily harmful, but the asymmetric political sympathy it produces is the recognizable TB-pattern.

### ADHD as "Superpower" or "Different Brain"

Over the past two decades, ADHD has acquired a distinctive romantic framing: not a disorder but a *cognitive style*, often paired with claims of creative advantage, entrepreneurial drive, or hyperfocus as a gift. Manifestations:

- "ADHD is a superpower" book and TED-talk genre
- The hyperfocus-as-asset framing
- Identification with entrepreneurial / inventive archetypes (the "scattered genius")

**Sontag test:** This framing reduces stigma (a real gain) but recodes symptoms as features and is most legible for high-functioning, economically supported patients. The framing rarely accommodates ADHD presenting as job loss, criminal-justice involvement, school failure, or substance use — outcomes more common in under-resourced populations with the same diagnosis. The romantic framing distributes sympathy unevenly even within a single diagnostic category.

### Autoimmune Disease as "Body Attacking Itself"

Autoimmune conditions (lupus, MS, rheumatoid arthritis, Crohn's, Hashimoto's) have acquired a metaphorical framing — *the body at war with itself* — that has both supportive and harmful aspects.

**Sontag test:** The framing **does not assign moral blame**, which would have been the punitive form ("you brought this on yourself with stress"). But it does aestheticize the disease as internal conflict, with the patient cast as battlefield. The "spoonie" identity (Christine Miserandino's spoon theory) is a partly romantic framing — solidarity-building and useful, but also creating an identity around chronic illness that can become a barrier to integration. As [[illness-narrative-and-coping]] notes, over-identifying with the sick role correlates with poorer outcomes.

### Cancer as "Warrior" / "Journey"

The cancer-as-battle narrative is one of the most analyzed contemporary metaphors (Sontag herself extended *Illness as Metaphor* with *AIDS and Its Metaphors* in 1989). Manifestations:

- "Battling cancer," "cancer warrior," "lost their battle"
- Pink ribbon culture as aesthetic and identity
- "Cancer changed my life for the better" survivor narratives

**Sontag test:** Sontag flagged this pattern in 1978 and it has only intensified. The warrior frame can be empowering (agency restoration — see [[illness-narrative-and-coping]]) but also implies that *losing* the battle reflects insufficient will. The pink-ribbon aesthetic concentrates resources on a particular framing of breast cancer that is more culturally legible than, e.g., metastatic, lung, or pancreatic cancer. Patient advocates within the breast cancer community have themselves objected to the romanticization of "survivorship" as obscuring metastatic patients who will not survive.

### Eating Disorders as "Discipline" or Aesthetic

Eating disorders — particularly anorexia nervosa — carry one of the most dangerous contemporary romanticizations because the symptoms (thinness, control of intake) are not just aestheticized but actively desired by the surrounding culture.

**Sontag test:** This is the closest modern analog to TB's "consumption is beauty" frame. Symptoms are read as virtue (discipline, willpower, beauty); the disease is associated with a high-status, educated, often female demographic; treatment access is uneven; cultural production (fashion, "thinspiration") actively reinforces the disease as ideal. Pro-ana online communities are the explicit, undisguised version of what TB's literary and salon culture did implicitly in the 19th century.

The harm here is especially acute because the symptoms themselves are reinforced by the romantic framing — losing weight produces social reward in a way that night sweats and hemoptysis never did.

### Bipolar Disorder as "Manic Genius"

The Kay Redfield Jamison *Touched with Fire* tradition — manic-depressive illness linked to artistic genius — is the bipolar version of the TB-as-artist's-disease frame.

**Sontag test:** The hypomanic episode (productivity, expansive thinking) is recoded as gift; the depressive episode and the destructive consequences of full mania (debt, broken relationships, hospitalization, suicide) are deprioritized in the public framing. The romance applies most legibly to artists with the resources to recover between episodes; it does not apply to patients with bipolar disorder who become homeless or incarcerated.

### Long COVID and Chronic Fatigue Conditions

A newer case, with both stigmatization (often dismissed as malingering or hypochondria, particularly in women) and emerging romantic counter-framings (the suffering high-achiever cut down at her peak). [[illness-narrative-and-coping]] applies: the political stakes are which framing wins, because that determines whether research funding and disability accommodation follow.

### Addictive Disorders — The Bohemian Romance

Substance use disorders, particularly involving opioids, alcohol, and stimulants, carry persistent romantic framings in artistic and literary culture — Beat Generation drug use, the rock-star overdose, the "tortured genius drinks himself to death" template (Hemingway, Fitzgerald, Kerouac).

**Sontag test:** Romance attaches to the famous and the productive; the same addiction in poor, non-white, or rural populations is criminalized rather than aestheticized. The unequal treatment of crack vs. powder cocaine in 1980s–90s U.S. drug policy is a textbook example of the same disease producing radically different cultural responses based on who has it.

## How Social Media Changes the Pattern

[[romanticization-of-illness]] flags this as a separate open question; it is so entangled with the contemporary romanticization landscape that it cannot be cleanly separated:

- **Aestheticization is faster and more visual.** TikTok and Instagram condense illness into images and short clips that select for visual legibility (thin bodies, tearful confessionals, "this is what depression looks like" aesthetics). The selection bias is stronger than in any prior medium.
- **Community formation is real and double-edged.** Patients with rare or stigmatized conditions can find peers in ways unavailable to 19th-century consumptives. This is a genuine gain (see [[illness-narrative-and-coping]] on community formation). It can also produce identity-stickiness around the diagnosis itself.
- **Self-diagnosis and "diagnosis influencers" reshape who claims an illness.** ADHD, autism, dissociative disorders, and chronic illness all show contemporary patterns of online communities expanding the population that identifies with the diagnosis, sometimes with limited clinical confirmation.
- **Algorithmic amplification rewards engagement, not accuracy.** The most viral illness content is rarely the most representative; the platform's incentives produce a romantic skew.

## The Test, Restated

The TB pattern from [[romanticization-of-illness]] is not "any positive framing of illness is harmful." The [[illness-narrative-and-coping]] resolution is that meaning-making, agency restoration, and community formation are legitimate uses of illness narrative. The harmful pattern is specifically:

> **Sympathy concentrated on the legible, productive, articulate, or aesthetically congruent subset of patients, with structural neglect of the rest.**

The contemporary cases above mostly satisfy this test. The lesson Green draws from TB applies to all of them: *how we narrate disease determines who is allowed to die*. Eating disorders make the point most starkly; depression and cancer make it most pervasively; ADHD and long COVID make it most quietly.

## Implications

- **Patient narrative tools** (the [[illness-narrative-and-coping]] argument) are not the problem and should not be sacrificed to fight romanticization.
- **Public discourse** about an illness needs auditing against the Sontag test: who does this framing make legible, and who does it erase?
- **Health-system accommodation** should track whether resources follow the romantic frame (depression treatment for the productively anxious) or the disease burden (severe depression where it co-occurs with poverty, addiction, housing instability).
- **Cultural production** — books, films, viral content — about illness is not neutral; its incentives are toward romance, and the corrective is conscious editorial counter-pressure.
