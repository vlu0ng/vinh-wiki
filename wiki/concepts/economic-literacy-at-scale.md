# Economic Literacy at Scale

The challenge of building broad public understanding of economics without the content being captured by partisan political narratives.

## The Problem

Economics education is contested territory. The same concepts — inflation, debt, monetary policy, trade — are systematically recruited into opposing political narratives. The Federal Reserve becomes either a protector of the dollar (right-populist framing) or a tool of financial elites (left-populist framing). Unemployment statistics are either proof of policy success or of rigged measurement depending on who's in office.

This politicization is not an accident. Economic outcomes affect material interests directly, so every political coalition has incentives to frame economic concepts in ways that serve its narrative. "Economic literacy" movements face the structural problem that their content will be appropriated and weaponized by both sides.

## What Works: Mechanistic Over Evaluative

Kyla Scanlon's approach in *In This Economy?* suggests a partial solution: anchor literacy in *mechanics* (how the system works) rather than *evaluation* (whether the system is good).

**More politically neutral:**
- How does the Federal Reserve set interest rates?
- What happens to the money supply when banks extend credit?
- Why do business cycles exist?
- How do expectations become self-fulfilling?

**More politically captured:**
- Should the Fed raise rates?
- Is the current level of government debt sustainable?
- Does immigration increase or decrease wages?

The mechanistic questions have empirically verifiable answers; the evaluative questions embed normative choices that map onto political positions. Economic literacy that stays in mechanistic territory is harder to weaponize.

## Behavioral Economics as Non-Partisan Ground

The behavioral economics literature is a relative safe harbor. Cognitive biases — loss aversion, availability bias, anchoring, social proof — are empirically documented, replicated across cultures, and do not map cleanly onto political positions. Everyone is subject to them; no political coalition "owns" the critique.

Scanlon's vibecession concept is an application of this: it's an empirical observation about the gap between data and sentiment, which is neutral about whether the sentiment is right or wrong. The rich/poor both experience sentiment; both can be in a vibecession.

## The Deeper Problem: Motivated Numeracy

Political scientist Dan Kahan's research on "motivated numeracy" shows that higher statistical literacy does not reduce political polarization on contested empirical questions — it *increases* it, because people use their analytical skills to find reasons to believe what their group already believes.

This suggests that more economic knowledge, absent other interventions, may not reduce political capture of economic framing. What Kahan's research points toward: the solution is not just knowledge, but *identity-decoupled* knowledge — learning mechanisms that don't trigger tribal identity threat responses.

Scanlon's storytelling approach is one attempt at this: by grounding economic concepts in personal narratives and everyday experience ("the economy is a bunch of people peopling around"), the concepts are less immediately retrievable as political weapons and more immediately legible as descriptions of shared experience.

## Institutional Design

Beyond individual pedagogy, the structural challenge requires institutional solutions:
- Journalism that separates mechanistic reporting from evaluative commentary
- Financial literacy curricula that are reviewed by ideologically diverse panels to detect capture
- Public economics communication (central bank communication, government reporting) that is honest about uncertainty rather than projecting false confidence

The Federal Reserve's communication strategy — deliberate vagueness (Fedspeak) — is itself a form of institutional design to prevent economic communication from immediately triggering market overreaction, though it trades political legibility for stability.

## The Honest Limit

No approach fully solves the problem. Economic concepts will always be contested because economic outcomes are unequally distributed and political coalitions form around those distributions. The goal of economic literacy cannot be to "depoliticize" economics — it is inherently political — but to equip people to distinguish mechanistic claims (how does X work) from evaluative claims (is X good), and to track their own motivated reasoning when they encounter economic information.
