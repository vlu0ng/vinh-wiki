# Pandemic Lessons from TB

What TB's 200-year history as a curable-but-killing disease reveals about the structural, political, and narrative dynamics that determine who survives pandemics — with direct application to COVID-19, HIV, and diseases yet to come.

## The Central Lesson: Technology Is Not the Bottleneck

TB has been curable since 1952. More than a million people died of it in 2023. COVID vaccines were developed within a year of the pandemic's onset; global vaccine distribution was radically unequal for two years. HIV antiretrovirals existed in the mid-1990s; millions in sub-Saharan Africa died unnecessarily through the early 2000s because drugs were unaffordable.

The pattern that [[tuberculosis-and-global-health]] establishes as TB's defining feature applies to all three diseases:

> *"The drugs are where the disease is not. And the disease is where the drugs are not."* — Dr. Peter Mugyenyi (on AIDS in 2000, quoted in *Everything Is Tuberculosis*)

This is not coincidence or bad luck. It is a structural feature of global health under conditions of radical wealth inequality. See [[health-equity]] for the framework.

## Lesson 1: Narrative Framing Determines Political Will

[[romanticization-of-illness]] documents how TB's romantic framing — the "artist's disease," a mark of sensitive genius — concentrated sympathy on middle-class consumptives while the urban poor (who bore the actual disease burden) were blamed for moral failing.

The same dynamic appears in COVID-19 and HIV:

- **HIV in the 1980s**: initial framings as a "gay plague" or disease of IV drug users redirected blame toward stigmatized communities, delaying research funding by years. The Reagan administration's refusal to publicly address AIDS is the most documented case of narrative framing causing mass death in modern American history.
- **COVID-19**: the framing of COVID deaths as concentrated among people with "underlying conditions" — a phrase that subtly locates cause in the individual rather than in systems of exposure, housing density, and healthcare access — reduced political urgency for protective measures among those who didn't identify with the "vulnerable."

The lesson: *how we frame illness determines who we fight for.* Sontag's prescription — strip moral meaning from disease, treat it as only biological — has direct political consequences. A disease that "happens to" people demands systemic response; a disease people "bring on themselves" does not.

## Lesson 2: Drug Resistance Follows Incomplete Treatment

TB's most dangerous current form — multi-drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) — developed because of incomplete treatment: patients who could not access or afford full drug courses took partial courses, which selected for resistant strains.

This exact mechanism appears in COVID and HIV:

- **HIV**: antiretroviral resistance emerges when patients take inconsistent courses, typically because of access barriers, not patient failure. [[partners-in-health]]'s community health worker model was developed specifically to address this: accompany patients through full treatment rather than distributing drugs and hoping for adherence.
- **COVID variants**: while COVID's rapid mutation is partly intrinsic to RNA viruses, sustained transmission in unvaccinated populations (primarily in low-income countries with poor vaccine access during 2021) provided the replication opportunities that produced the most vaccine-evading variants. Global vaccination is an individual-country interest, not just a global charity.

The structural lesson: **access failures don't just harm the people denied treatment — they generate threats that rebound on the well-resourced.**

## Lesson 3: Cures Without Distribution Systems Are Not Cures

One TB-specific lesson that generalizes: the bottleneck is rarely the drug. It is the infrastructure to deliver it — trained health workers, cold chains, diagnostic capacity, community trust.

[[partners-in-health]] pioneered in Haiti and Sierra Leone the insight that vertical disease-specific programs (TB-only clinics, HIV-only programs) were less effective than building underlying health system capacity. COVID revealed the same gap globally: countries without strong primary care infrastructure struggled to run vaccination programs even when doses were available.

The lesson for future pandemics: **health system investment is pandemic preparedness.** Treating infrastructure investment as ordinary development aid rather than security spending is a strategic error that TB's history repeats, disease by disease.

## Lesson 4: The Moral Weight of Preventable Death Is Cumulative

John Green's argument in *Everything Is Tuberculosis* is moral as well as epidemiological: allowing a curable disease to kill 1.25 million people a year, for 70 years after a cure existed, is not a natural tragedy. It is a sustained political choice.

Each pandemic tests whether we've internalized this lesson. COVID's global distribution failures suggest we haven't. The WHO's "End TB" target of 2030 was set in 2015 and is currently off-track.

The structural question [[health-equity]] poses: can global health equity be achieved without wealth redistribution? TB's history suggests no — access to drugs is downstream of access to resources. Incremental technical solutions (cheaper generic drugs, faster diagnostics) help but don't resolve the underlying inequality that makes *"the drugs are where the disease is not"* a stable equilibrium.

## Summary: The Four Transferable Lessons

| Lesson | TB evidence | COVID parallel | HIV parallel |
|---|---|---|---|
| Technology ≠ access | Curable 1952, still kills millions | Vaccines developed fast; distributed unequally | ARVs existed; unaffordable in Africa for a decade |
| Narrative framing is political | Romantic myth → neglect of the poor | "Underlying conditions" → deprioritization of vulnerable | "Gay plague" → delayed federal response |
| Incomplete treatment creates resistance | MDR-TB from partial drug courses | COVID variants from unvaccinated populations | ARV resistance from inconsistent access |
| Systems, not just drugs, are needed | Community health workers essential | Vaccination required health infrastructure | Adherence required accompaniment, not just prescriptions |
