# Stoic Virtues

The Stoics identified four *cardinal virtues* as the complete framework for living well. Virtue, in the Stoic sense,
is not a feeling or disposition but a *practice* — something you do, not something you have.

## The Four Cardinals

1. **Temperance** (*sophrosyne*) — self-regulation, moderation, the ability to control appetites and impulses
2. **Courage** (*andreia*) — action despite fear; moral as well as physical courage
3. **Wisdom** (*phronesis*) — practical judgment; seeing situations clearly; knowing what to do
4. **Justice** (*dikaiosyne*) — right action toward others; fairness; honesty; the social expression of virtue

Marcus Aurelius's formulation (underlined by Harry Truman): *"The four greatest virtues are moderation, wisdom,
justice, and fortitude, and if a man is able to cultivate those, that's all he needs to live a happy and successful
life."*

## The Social Turn

Early Stoicism emphasized individual self-sufficiency. As the philosophy moved from Greek philosophy schools to Roman
civic life (Cicero, Seneca, Marcus Aurelius), it became increasingly other-directed. Justice, the most social of the
virtues, was central to this evolution.

Historian's note: the Stoics who are remembered — Cato, Marcus, Seneca (despite his contradictions) — were defined
by their public service, not just private equanimity.

## Virtue as Its Own Reward

The Stoic claim: virtue is the *only* good; everything else (wealth, health, reputation) is a "preferred
indifferent" — valuable, but not required for the good life. Truman: *"I have believed in honor, ethics, and right
living as its own reward."*

## Examples / Case Studies

- **Truman**: decades of virtue practice before power; character as the infrastructure of leadership
- **Frank Robinson** ([[stoic-justice]]): fined himself after a lapse, unprompted
- **Whistleblowers**: truth-telling as justice in action, even at personal cost
