# Latticework Feedback Loops Outside Investing

What the minimum viable feedback loop looks like for building [[latticework-pedagogy|latticework]] reasoning in domains other than investing — where there is no equivalent of a market price to score your judgment cheaply, daily, and unambiguously.

## Why This Is the Hard Problem

[[latticework-pedagogy]] argues that the latticework is built less by reading and more by *deliberate application with feedback*. [[charlie-munger|Munger's]] feedback substrate was unusual: market prices, marked to market daily, accumulated over 60+ years, with money on the line. The market is a near-ideal feedback signal — objective, fast (intraday and quarterly), unforgiving, and impossible to rationalize away.

Most other domains lack any single signal with all four properties. A doctor sees outcomes weeks or years later, often after the patient has changed clinicians. A teacher rarely knows whether a student's later success traced to their class. A policy analyst can wait decades for a clear verdict. A software engineer gets fast feedback on whether code runs but slow, noisy feedback on whether the design was wise. Parenting feedback arrives in 20-year increments.

The question is not whether feedback exists in these domains — it does — but **what is the minimum structure that makes the feedback usable for latticework formation, given that no single signal is sufficient.**

## The Minimum Structure

From the conditions identified in [[disposition-building-practices]], a viable non-investing feedback loop needs four properties; in the absence of a market, they must be assembled deliberately rather than inherited from the domain:

1. **Pre-commitment.** A written prediction or written reasoning before the outcome is known. Without this, hindsight bias erases the feedback signal.
2. **A resolvable proxy.** Some outcome that *will* be observable on a useful timescale, even if it is only a partial measure of the underlying question.
3. **A scheduled reckoning.** A calendar appointment with your own prior reasoning. The dispositional benefit is in the moment of being wrong on the record.
4. **Cross-domain variation.** Predictions or decisions spanning more than one model area, so that calibration in one domain doesn't masquerade as general wisdom.

The minimal viable loop is therefore: **a written decision (or forecast) with the explicit reasoning, a chosen resolution date on a proxy you have committed to in advance, and a scheduled review on that date.** This is the [[disposition-building-practices]] minimum applied to non-market contexts.

## Domain-Specific Resolvable Proxies

The single hardest design problem in non-investing domains is choosing the proxy. The proxy must be *resolvable* (you can observe whether you were right) and *non-trivial* (being right about the proxy actually pressures the underlying model). Some workable examples:

| Domain | Long-horizon question | Resolvable proxy (months, not years) |
|---|---|---|
| Medicine (clinical) | Did my diagnosis hold up? | Test result, response to treatment within 4–12 weeks |
| Software architecture | Was this design right? | Specific predicted failure modes (latency, on-call pages, refactor difficulty) at 90 days |
| Teaching | Did this student learn? | Quiz / project performance at end of unit; one-year retention check via a single recall question |
| Writing | Will this argument hold up? | Reactions of three pre-selected smart readers at fixed dates |
| Policy / management | Will this initiative work? | Stated leading indicators with explicit thresholds at 30/90/180 days |
| Forecasting (general) | Is my world model calibrated? | Public forecasting platforms (Good Judgment Open, Metaculus) — see [[superforecasting]] |
| Hiring | Was this hire correct? | Written prediction of 12-month performance review rating, sealed at offer time |
| Parenting | Is this approach working? | Narrow behavioral predictions on month-scale (e.g., "screen time rule will produce X within 6 weeks"); 20-year questions cannot be resolved |

Each proxy is partial. None is the market. But each makes the underlying mental model testable on a timescale short enough for habit formation.

## Why Forecasting Tournaments Are the Closest Analog

[[superforecasting|Forecasting tournaments]] (Good Judgment Open, Metaculus, internal corporate forecasting) come closest to replicating the market's feedback properties in non-financial domains:

- Pre-commitment is enforced (timestamped probability)
- The resolution criterion is fixed in advance
- Scoring is mechanical (Brier score), removing rationalization
- Questions span domains, forcing cross-disciplinary model use
- Cost of participation is low

[[superforecasting]] notes the limit: tournament practice builds calibration and aggregation, not full far-transfer. But for the *feedback-loop* property specifically — the thing missing in most non-investing domains — forecasting tournaments are the most accessible substitute available.

## The Substitute When No Proxy Is Available

For genuinely long-horizon questions (parenting outcomes, policy impacts, life choices), no monthly proxy exists. The substitute is **process review without outcome resolution**:

- Record the reasoning, the considered alternatives, the expected outcome.
- Schedule a review not at "outcome" but at *update points* — when new information arrives that should have changed the prediction.
- Score yourself on whether you actually updated, not on whether you were right.

This is weaker than a market signal because it cannot test the model against reality. It can, however, test the model against *new evidence*, which is the second-best feedback structure and the one Tetlock identifies as a distinguishing superforecaster habit.

## The Munger-Outside-Investing Cases

Munger himself ran several non-investing latticework experiments. They illuminate the substitute structures:

- **Munger, Tolles & Olson** — feedback from case outcomes and client retention; slower and noisier than markets but still real.
- **Harvard-Westlake School and Good Samaritan Hospital boards** — institutional decisions whose consequences play out over years; here Munger relied heavily on the partnership/peer-review structure (Buffett conversations, board discussions) as a feedback proxy for outcomes too slow to score directly.
- **Architecture and real estate** — physical buildings either work or do not on a 1–5 year horizon; concrete and slower than equities but still resolvable.

Across all three, Munger compensated for the absence of a market by *creating institutions of peer challenge* — board structures, partnership conversations, public talks that exposed his reasoning to critique. Peer-challenge structures are the cheapest substitute for market signals and are available in any domain.

## The Minimum Viable Set (Recommendation)

For someone building latticework reasoning outside investing, the minimum viable feedback structure is:

1. **A decision journal** with written reasoning and an explicit, pre-committed proxy outcome on a 30 / 90 / 180-day horizon. (From [[disposition-building-practices]].)
2. **A scheduled monthly review** of journal entries whose horizons have elapsed, with a written entry on which mental model failed or held.
3. **A forecasting tournament account** (Good Judgment Open, Metaculus, or an internal team forecast) for cross-domain calibration on questions that don't intersect your day job — this provides the cross-domain variation the journal alone may not.
4. **A peer-challenge structure** — one to three readers / partners who see the reasoning before the outcome and are licensed to push back. This is the cheapest substitute for the Buffett-Munger conversational discipline.

The first three are individual. The fourth is institutional and is the most often skipped, but the [[charlie-munger|Munger]] case suggests it carries disproportionate weight.

## What This Does Not Replace

The substitute structures cannot replicate:

- **The amount of skin in the game** that investing provides. Money concentrates attention in a way that journal entries do not.
- **The long horizon and compounding** that produced Munger's specific judgment. The minimum viable loop builds calibration; it does not, in a single decade, build the cross-disciplinary intuition that 60 years of partner conversation produced.
- **Selection effects.** As [[disposition-vs-knowledge]] notes, practices supplement environment but do not replace the dispositional substrate Munger brought to age 30 (see [[munger-disposition-formation]]).

What the minimum viable loop *does* deliver is the feedback signal — the part of latticework formation that is most often missing in non-investing domains and that no amount of reading replaces.
