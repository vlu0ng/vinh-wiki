# Crony Capitalism

An economic system in which market outcomes are systematically distorted by the capture of state institutions — especially the central bank, regulatory agencies, and the legislative process — by private financial interests.

## Key Properties

- **State capture** — private actors (banks, corporations, lobbies) gain outsized influence over the rules of the game, including tax codes, bailout decisions, and regulatory standards
- **Socialized losses, privatized gains** — profits accrue to connected private actors; losses (through bailouts, subsidies, or lax enforcement) are absorbed by taxpayers or the public
- **Regulatory capture** — agencies intended to police industries come to serve them instead; the "revolving door" between Wall Street and Washington is a key mechanism
- **[[bubble-finance|Bubble finance]] enablement** — crony capitalism and loose monetary policy are mutually reinforcing: central banks inflate asset prices, enriching the financial sector, which then lobbies to keep the conditions in place

## Stockman's Application

[[david-stockman|David Stockman]] uses "crony capitalism" to describe the bipartisan Washington consensus from Nixon through Obama: bailouts of Wall Street (TARP), AIG, and the auto industry; the Fed's systematic inflation of asset prices; and K Street lobbying that shapes tax and spending policy to favor connected industries. He argues this is distinct from genuine free markets, which would have allowed insolvent institutions to fail.

## Distinctions

- **Crony capitalism vs. free markets** — in a genuine free market, losses discipline bad decisions; in crony capitalism, losses are externalized
- **Crony capitalism vs. socialism** — both distort markets, but crony capitalism maintains the form of private ownership while socializing risk
- **Crony capitalism vs. corruption** — corruption is individual and transactional; crony capitalism is systemic, embedded in institutions
