# Disposition-Building Practices

A set of practices — primarily journaling, forecasting, and decision review — that empirical and philosophical evidence suggests can narrow the [[disposition-vs-knowledge|disposition/knowledge gap]] by installing feedback loops that knowledge transmission alone cannot provide.

## Why Practices Matter More Than Content

The core problem from [[disposition-vs-knowledge]]: knowledge can be stated, tested, and transmitted through text, but disposition is a stable behavioral orientation formed through *habituation under conditions of feedback*. The four mechanisms that prevent knowledge from becoming disposition all have one feature in common: **absent or delayed feedback**.

Practices that reliably build disposition share a structural property: they force real predictions into confrontation with real outcomes, creating the feedback signal that changes behavior.

## The Three Core Practices

### 1. Forecasting

Forecasting — assigning explicit probabilities to specific, time-bound predictions and then reviewing outcomes — is the most rigorously studied of the three practices.

**Why it works structurally:**
- Forces the practitioner to *commit* to a belief with enough precision that it can be falsified
- Creates a record that makes later rationalization harder
- Provides clear, low-latency feedback (the event either happened or it didn't)
- Rewards calibration (accurate uncertainty) rather than mere accuracy, reducing overconfidence

Philip Tetlock's superforecaster research (referenced in [[latticework-pedagogy]]) found that people who engage in structured forecasting develop habits of active open-mindedness, model comparison, and evidence-based updating — habits that closely overlap with the dispositional requirements of the latticework approach. See [[superforecasting]] for the concept in detail.

**The key: feedback with accountability.** Forecasting without review is not forecasting — it is opining. The dispositional benefit comes from the *moment of reckoning*, when you must explain why you were wrong.

### 2. Decision Journaling

Decision journaling — recording the reasoning, evidence, and expected outcomes at the moment of a decision, before the outcome is known — addresses a structural weakness in ordinary experience: **hindsight bias**. Without a contemporaneous record, memory rewrites past reasoning to match outcomes, eliminating the feedback signal.

**What to capture:**
- The decision context (what was at stake, what information was available)
- The reasoning process (which models or frameworks were applied, which were considered and rejected)
- The explicit prediction (what outcome is expected and why)
- The emotional state (to detect when intuition was noise vs. signal)

**Why it builds disposition:**
- Makes the reasoning visible and reviewable, not just the outcome
- Creates a longitudinal record that reveals personal cognitive patterns (repeated overconfidence in domain X, consistent availability bias in Y)
- The awareness of being recorded creates a mild accountability effect that slightly improves decision-making in real time

From [[latticework-pedagogy]]: "The models that stick in Munger's usage are the ones he has seen both succeed and fail in real situations." Decision journaling is how you see both.

### 3. Decision Review

Decision review — revisiting past decisions after outcomes are known, with the journal record as a check against hindsight revision — closes the feedback loop that journaling opens.

**Effective review asks:**
- Was the reasoning sound given what was knowable at the time? (Process, not just outcome)
- Which mental models proved most useful? Which were misapplied?
- What would have changed the decision? ([[inversion|Inversion]])

The [[mental-models-and-multidisciplinary-thinking|Munger]] emphasis on thinking about *failure* is natural here: reviewing decisions where models failed is more instructive than reviewing successes, because success is more easily attributed to luck.

## The Minimal Viable Set

Based on the evidence in [[latticework-pedagogy]], a minimal viable practice set needs to satisfy three conditions:

| Condition | Why | Practice that provides it |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-commitment | Prevents hindsight revision | Decision journal + forecasting |
| Clear feedback signal | Required for habit formation | Forecasting review; decision review |
| Cross-domain application | Required for latticework, not just calibration | Apply all three across domains, not just one |

The minimum viable set is therefore: **a decision journal with explicit probability estimates for key predictions, reviewed quarterly**. The quarterly review is the essential step most people skip.

## What These Practices Cannot Do

The practices work within the limits defined by [[disposition-vs-knowledge]]:

- They do **not** replace the long time horizons and high stakes that Munger's actual investing experience provided
- They cannot install cross-disciplinary curiosity if that trait is absent — they can only develop the *expression* of curiosity that already exists
- They are significantly more effective for people already motivated by accuracy than for people motivated by social validation

The evidence from character education research: **modeling by exemplars and institutional culture** remain stronger predictors of dispositional change than any structured practice alone. Practices supplement but do not replace environment.
