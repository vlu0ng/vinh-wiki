# Social Media and Illness Aesthetics

How Instagram, TikTok, and platform culture have transformed the aestheticization of illness — amplifying some of what Susan Sontag warned against, while also creating new forms of solidarity that the pre-social-media landscape couldn't provide.

## What Changes Under Social Media

Sontag's *Illness as Metaphor* (1978) identified romanticization as a literary and cultural phenomenon embedded in 19th-century tuberculosis narratives. Social media has changed the mechanism and scale of illness aestheticization in several ways:

**From passive consumption to active self-presentation.** The 19th-century romanticization was applied to illness *from outside* by cultural observers (Keats dying poetically, as narrated by critics and biographers). Social media inverts this: the sick person is now their own narrator, curating and performing their illness identity for an audience. This gives patients unprecedented agency in shaping illness narratives — and simultaneously creates new pressures around how illness should look.

**Visual media aestheticizes the body.** Illness was primarily aestheticized through language in earlier eras (descriptions of the consumptive's pallor and feverish eyes). Instagram and TikTok make the body visible. The "sick girl aesthetic" — clean hospital beds, IV bags photographed with golden-hour light, carefully chosen playlists, beautiful bedside arrangements — creates an aspirational visual vocabulary for illness that photographs bear a suspicious resemblance to wellness content.

**Algorithmic amplification rewards emotional resonance.** Platform algorithms optimize for engagement, which rewards content that produces strong emotional responses. Illness content that is beautiful, dramatic, or inspiring generates more engagement than content that is mundane, ugly, or ambivalent. This creates a systematic filter: the illness experiences that become visible on social media are disproportionately the photogenic, the narratively coherent, and the recoverable — a distorting selection effect.

**Community formation and illness identity.** Social media enables patients with rare or misunderstood conditions to find each other. The "spoonie community" (a reference to Christine Miserandino's Spoon Theory for chronic illness), "chronically ill TikTok," and similar communities provide genuine support, information, and belonging that isolated patients desperately need. This is an unambiguous good. The complication: community formation also produces *illness identity* — a self-concept organized around being sick — which can, in some cases, make illness harder to leave behind.

## New Romanticizations Social Media Has Created or Amplified

**Depression as creative sensitivity.** The "sad girl aesthetic" on Tumblr (2010s) and its successors aestheticized depression, anxiety, and self-harm in ways that blurred the line between art and suffering. Images of empty rooms, empty highways, and emotional numbness circulated alongside quotes about exhaustion and meaninglessness. The aesthetic became a form of social currency — being depressed in the right way was legible as sensitivity. Platforms eventually responded with content moderation (Tumblr's self-harm content policies, Instagram's restrictions on eating disorder content), acknowledging that the aestheticization was actively harmful.

**ADHD and autism as "neurodivergent identity."** TikTok's algorithm-driven "neurodivergent community" has genuinely destigmatized ADHD and autism — making it easier for people who were undiagnosed or ashamed of their diagnosis to understand themselves and seek accommodation. The complication is the aestheticization of cognitive difference as quirky, creative, and authentic: the ADHD identity on TikTok tends to emphasize creative impulsivity and hyperfocus while underemphasizing the disruptive and sometimes debilitating aspects of the condition. The "neurodivergent" framing risks producing a community organized around identity rather than treatment, which can delay or complicate care.

**Long COVID and contested illness.** Social media communities around long COVID, ME/CFS, and similar conditions have served a crucial function: providing documentation, solidarity, and pressure that helped push conditions from "unexplained" to recognized. The advocacy role of patient social media communities in legitimizing contested illnesses represents a structural improvement over the pre-social-media era, where isolated patients were more easily dismissed. The aestheticization risk here is lower because these communities emerged primarily in opposition to medical dismissal, which tends to produce political framing rather than aesthetic framing.

## The Doubleness: What Social Media Gets Right

The Sontag critique cannot be straightforwardly applied to social media illness culture without acknowledging what has genuinely improved:

- **Destigmatization at scale.** Mental health stigma has measurably declined among young people who grew up on social media, partly because illness became legible as normal rather than shameful. Saying "I have depression" or "I have an eating disorder" on social media — and receiving community response rather than shame — has changed the social meaning of these disclosures.

- **Patient expertise and advocacy.** Chronic illness communities online have become repositories of practical knowledge — medication side effects, accommodation strategies, diagnostic pathways — that patients often know better than generalist physicians. This is a structural improvement in health information access.

- **Community for rare conditions.** Before social media, a patient with a rare condition might spend years feeling uniquely broken. Social media collapses geographic distance, making community formation possible for conditions where no local community exists.

## The Persistent Sontag Problem

What social media doesn't change: the fundamental problem Sontag identified — that aestheticizing illness redistributes sympathy unevenly. Social media illness culture continues to favor:
- Conditions that photograph well (visible suffering, chronic but not terminal)
- Patients with social capital to produce polished content
- Narratives of struggle-toward-recovery over narratives of permanent limitation
- Illnesses that are legible as identity (neurodivergence, chronic pain) over illnesses that are stigmatized (addiction, severe mental illness with violent expression)

The algorithmic amplification of aesthetically resonant illness content reproduces and potentially amplifies the selectivity Sontag described. Social media didn't invent the aestheticization of illness; it gave everyone a platform to participate in it, with algorithmic incentives determining which aesthetics win.
