# Limits of Scanlon's Economic Optimism

Kyla Scanlon's framing in *In This Economy?* premises that economies trend toward okay-ness over time. This is empirically grounded in historical business-cycle data but faces two structural challenges: distributional inequality and climate risk.

## The Claim Being Challenged

Scanlon's optimism is built on two pillars:
1. **Business cycles smooth out**: economies expand and contract but trend upward over sufficiently long horizons
2. **Human resilience**: the economy is "a bunch of people peopling around" — adaptive, creative, capable of adjustment

This framing is partly a corrective to doom-optimizing financial media, which systematically overstates the probability of catastrophe because alarm sells. Scanlon is right that media-induced pessimism is often miscalibrated.

## The Distributional Challenge

The business-cycle-trends-up claim is an aggregate. Aggregates hide distribution — the economy can trend upward while large segments of the population trend downward.

**K-shaped recovery (COVID-era):** after 2020, upper-income households recovered quickly (asset prices rose, remote work was viable, savings increased); lower-income households faced persistent wage pressure, healthcare costs, and housing inflation. The aggregate economic indicators showed recovery; the lived experience for many did not.

**Piketty's r > g argument:** when returns to capital (r) consistently exceed economic growth (g), inequality compounds over time even in growing economies. Wealth concentrates structurally, not just cyclically. If this is correct, the "trend toward okay-ness" applies to the owners of capital and may not apply to those selling labor.

**The vibecession reframing:** Scanlon coined "vibecession" to describe the gap between positive aggregate data and negative popular sentiment — implying the sentiment is somewhat irrational. But some of the gap may be rational: people in the bottom half of the income distribution may be accurately reading their own economy (rising rents, stagnant wages, healthcare cost inflation) even as the aggregate looks healthy. The vibecession is partly a statistical artifact of using mean rather than median experience.

## The Climate Risk Challenge

Secular environmental trends introduce a more fundamental challenge: they don't follow business-cycle dynamics and have no automatic recovery mechanism.

**Physical climate risk:**
- Agricultural disruption from extreme heat, drought, and flooding affects food supply chains and price stability
- Mass displacement from coastal flooding and climate-driven conflict represents a structural economic shock with no cyclical recovery
- Supply chain disruptions from climate events (Hurricane Helene's impact on Asheville's manufacturing; Texas freeze's impact on semiconductors) are accelerating

**Transition risk:**
- The shift away from fossil fuels involves stranded assets worth trillions of dollars
- Energy cost volatility during transition creates macroeconomic uncertainty that doesn't fit the "temporary disruption, then trend" model

**The limit of optimism:** business-cycle analysis works on assumptions of system continuity — the same productive infrastructure, roughly similar climate conditions, stable population. Climate risk introduces the possibility of structural discontinuity: prolonged agricultural failure, mass displacement, or energy system disruption that does not self-correct within a business-cycle timeframe.

## What Scanlon's Optimism Gets Right

The critique does not invalidate Scanlon's core contribution:
- Normal economic fluctuations (recession, inflation, monetary tightening) are systematically overweighted as catastrophic by financial media
- Human adaptability is underweighted in doom narratives
- The behavioral and emotional components of economic experience are real and important

The scope condition is: *within normal business-cycle dynamics, in aggregate, for developed economies with historical institutions intact.* The optimism is well-calibrated for that domain. It becomes poorly calibrated when applied to distributional questions, secular inequality trends, or long-horizon climate scenarios.
