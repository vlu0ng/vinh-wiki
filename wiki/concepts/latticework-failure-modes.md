# Latticework Failure Modes

The categories of problems that resist [[mental-models|multi-model synthesis]] — where Munger's [[mental-models-and-multidisciplinary-thinking|latticework approach]] fails, misleads, or is simply insufficient.

## Overview

The latticework is a tool for *judgment under uncertainty in complex, slow-feedback domains*. It was developed in the context of investment analysis and business evaluation — domains with rich historical patterns, legible incentive structures, and time horizons long enough for models to play out. Several categories of problems fall outside these conditions.

## Five Categories Where Latticework Falls Short

### 1. Deep Procedural Expertise
The latticework is a tool for judgment, not technical execution. Differential equations, surgical technique, bench biology, advanced software engineering, and similar skills require deliberate practice and domain-specific feedback that no amount of cross-disciplinary reading can replace. Munger himself is clear about this: he operates through others who have technical expertise; he provides judgment about whether to deploy it and in what direction, not the execution itself.

The failure mode: overconfident generalists who believe their breadth substitutes for technical depth in domains requiring both. The latticework *complements* domain expertise; it does not replace it.

### 2. Model Conflict Without Resolution
The framework doesn't specify how to weight competing models when they point in different directions. Munger's preferred answer is "wait for the lollapalooza" — act only when multiple models align. This works in patient, low-frequency decision contexts (Berkshire's investment pace). In higher-frequency contexts with forced decisions, model conflict is a genuine problem the framework leaves unresolved.

The failure mode: paralysis when models disagree, or post-hoc rationalization where the preferred answer gets "confirmed" by cherry-picking which models to apply.

### 3. Truly Novel Domains
In domains where no settled models yet exist — early LLMs (2019-2020), mRNA vaccines pre-COVID, the early internet — applying familiar frameworks from adjacent fields can be *actively misleading*. Analogies to previous technological transitions may miscalibrate both speed and nature of change.

Overconfident polymaths are a real phenomenon: the same breadth that enables insight in established domains can generate spurious analogies in genuinely novel ones. The latticework is most powerful when applied to domains with long historical records (business competition, human psychology, engineering tradeoffs); it is least reliable when applied to domains that are structurally unprecedented.

### 4. Fast-Iteration, High-Pressure Environments
The latticework is a deliberate, reflective process. In emergency medicine, competitive athletics, active trading, or operational crisis response, tacit expertise built through high-volume practice (Kahneman's System 1, Dreyfus's "expert" level) typically outperforms conscious model consultation. The cognitive overhead of model retrieval and synthesis is unavailable under time pressure.

The resolution: the latticework is valuable for *preparation* in these domains (building pattern libraries, designing protocols, understanding failure modes in advance) but not for *in-the-moment* decision-making. Surgeons who've built mental models of complication patterns recognize them faster; the models are still operating, but they're encoded in tacit perception, not explicit retrieval.

### 5. Irreducibly Political Problems
Mental models can diagnose why institutions resist change (incentive-caused bias, commitment and consistency, social proof, etc.) but cannot determine whether change will happen or how to force it. The best models provide structural analysis of power dynamics and entrenched interest; they don't specify action in political contexts where coalition-building, timing, and contingency dominate.

The failure mode: a well-modeled analysis of "why this institution is broken" that doesn't translate into effective action because the models describe dynamics without supplying leverage points.

## The Meta-Point: The Latticework Has a Domain

The latticework is an excellent general-purpose tool for *reflective judgment in complex, established domains* by *individuals operating across long time horizons*. Its failure modes are mostly predictable expansions of those constraints: strip out the reflective time, remove the established patterns, or enter a domain requiring technical execution rather than judgment, and the approach's advantages diminish or reverse.

Munger himself exhibits characteristic competence within these bounds: he consistently defers to technical experts (geologists, scientists, engineers) and focuses his own model-application on the business and human dimensions where his approach is genuinely powerful.
