# Growth Mindset

A psychological framework developed by Stanford psychologist **[[carol-dweck|Carol Dweck]]** describing the belief that intelligence, talent, and ability are **developable** through effort, good strategies, and learning from mistakes — as opposed to being fixed traits you either have or don't.

## Fixed vs. Growth Mindset

| Fixed Mindset | Growth Mindset |
|---|---|
| "I'm just not a math person." | "I can't do this *yet*." |
| Avoids challenges (might expose limits) | Embraces challenges (opportunity to grow) |
| Sees effort as a sign of weakness | Sees effort as the path to mastery |
| Ignores useful feedback | Learns from criticism |
| Threatened by others' success | Inspired by others' success |

## The Word "Yet"

Dweck's most famous classroom intervention: when a student says "I can't do this," the teacher responds "You can't do this *yet.*" The word "yet" repositions failure as a stage in a process rather than a verdict on ability.

## Neuroscience Foundation

- Synaptic connections form and strengthen through use — this is neuroplasticity
- Challenging tasks, especially those involving errors noticed and corrected, produce the most synaptic growth
- Fixed mindset thinking is partly driven by cultural messages received in childhood

## Application to Mathematics

[[jo-boaler|Jo Boaler]] extended Dweck's framework specifically to mathematics, arguing that math is uniquely afflicted by fixed-mindset culture:
- Speed equated with intelligence
- Single right answers
- Early ability tracking
- Timed tests that activate threat responses

Full synthesis: [[mathematics-education]]

## Critiques and Nuance

- Growth mindset interventions have had mixed results in large-scale replications
- Critics argue it can be used to shift blame from systemic failures onto individual students ("just try harder")
- Dweck herself emphasizes that mindset alone is insufficient without structural support (good teaching, adequate resources)
