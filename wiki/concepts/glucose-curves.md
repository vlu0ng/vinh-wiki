# Glucose Curves

The visual/conceptual model introduced by Jessie Inchauspée to describe how blood glucose levels change over time in
response to food, activity, and other inputs. The goal is not to minimize glucose but to **flatten the curves** —
reduce the amplitude of spikes and crashes.

## The Basic Pattern

- **Spike**: Rapid rise in blood glucose after eating (especially refined carbohydrates and sugars)
- **Crash**: Subsequent rapid decline, often below baseline, triggering hunger, fatigue, and cravings
- **Flat curve**: Gradual, modest rise and return to baseline — associated with sustained energy and stable mood

## Why Flattening Matters

Repeated large spikes:
- Cause repeated large insulin releases → insulin resistance over time
- Drive fat storage (especially visceral fat)
- Disrupt hormonal signaling (including reproductive hormones — PCOS, missed periods)
- Cause oxidative stress and inflammation
- Are implicated in type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, fatty liver disease

## The Downstream Cascade

A critical insight: because fructose is co-packaged with glucose in almost all foods, and because insulin is released
in response to glucose, **flattening the glucose curve automatically flattens fructose and insulin curves**. This
makes glucose the highest-leverage single metabolic metric to monitor and manage.

## How to Flatten

Key interventions (from [[glucose-revolution]]):
- **Food order**: fiber → protein/fat → starches/sugars (slows gastric emptying, blunts spike)
- **Vinegar before meals**: acetic acid slows glucose absorption
- **Movement after eating**: muscle contractions absorb glucose without insulin
- **Savory breakfast**: avoids a morning spike that cascades throughout the day
- **Dessert timing**: sweets after a meal cause smaller spikes than sweets on an empty stomach
- **Avoid liquid glucose**: juices and sweet drinks spike faster than solid food

## Monitoring Tool: CGM

Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGMs) — wearable sensors that track blood glucose every few minutes — democratized the
ability to see one's own glucose curves. Inchauspée's work is partly built on CGM self-experimentation.

## Caveats

Glucose curves are one important metabolic signal but not the only one. Ultra-processed oils, alcohol, sleep
deprivation, and chronic stress all affect health through glucose-independent pathways.
