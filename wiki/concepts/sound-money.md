# Sound Money

A monetary system in which the currency is disciplined by a commodity anchor (gold) or strict rules that prevent the central bank from inflating asset prices or monetizing government deficits at will.

## Key Properties

- **Commodity anchor** — historically, gold; money is redeemable at a fixed rate, preventing unlimited expansion of the money supply
- **Fiscal discipline** — when borrowing must be financed at market rates without central bank support, governments face real budget constraints
- **Price signals** — sound money preserves the information content of prices; distorted monetary policy corrupts price signals and misdirects investment
- **Debt discipline** — insolvency is real; creditors and investors bear the consequences of bad bets rather than receiving bailouts

## Historical Context

- **Bretton Woods (1944–1971)** — post-WWII international monetary system anchored to gold via the dollar; provided relative stability and the "golden age" of postwar growth
- **Nixon's 1971 decision** — closing the gold window ended dollar-gold convertibility and launched the era of floating fiat currencies; from Stockman's perspective, this was the original sin of modern monetary deformation
- **The Volcker shock (1979–1982)** — Paul Volcker's aggressive rate hikes to break inflation represent the last exercise of sound money discipline in Stockman's view; largely abandoned since
- **Post-2008 QE** — the Federal Reserve's quantitative easing programs mark, for sound money advocates, the apex of monetary deformation

## Criticism of the Concept

Critics argue that gold-standard rigidity caused the Great Depression by preventing monetary expansion when it was needed. They also note that "sound money" regimes often created severe deflation and financial panics in the 19th century. Modern monetary theory (MMT) represents the polar opposite view: that currency-issuing governments face no fundamental financing constraint.
