# Health Equity Without Redistribution

Whether meaningful progress on global health equity requires large-scale wealth transfer from rich to poor countries — or whether targeted institutional and policy interventions can close gaps without redistribution.

## The Question

Health inequity is causally downstream of structural poverty. If the root cause is economic, the natural prescription is economic: redistribute wealth, and health follows. But this prescription faces political barriers so high as to make it nearly useless as actionable guidance. The more tractable question: how much health equity can be achieved *without* waiting for redistribution?

## Where Redistribution Is Not Required

### Targeted Infectious Disease

The strongest case that redistribution isn't necessary: diseases with cheap, available cures. TB kills 1.25 million people annually despite being curable since 1952 with drugs costing $20–40 for a full course. The gap is not wealth but *allocation, IP constraints, and delivery systems*.

- **Compulsory licensing and TRIPS flexibilities** allow low-income countries to produce generic versions of patented drugs without requiring wealth transfers — as Brazil demonstrated with antiretrovirals in the early 2000s
- **Community health worker models** (Partners in Health's Haitian program, Ethiopia's extension workers) dramatically improved TB and HIV outcomes in low-income settings without requiring countries to become wealthy first
- **Thailand's TRIPS flexibilities** on second-line ARVs in 2006–2007 expanded treatment access without significant wealth redistribution

The mechanism: institutional reform (IP law, healthcare delivery architecture) can decouple drug access from wealth level.

### Primary Care Infrastructure

Countries with strong primary care systems consistently outperform wealthier countries on health metrics per dollar. Sri Lanka, Cuba, Thailand, and Kerala (India) achieve health outcomes comparable to much wealthier countries. The mechanism is institutional investment, not wealth.

What these cases share: universal primary care, community-based prevention, and basic public health infrastructure. These are achievable at middle-income GDP levels and don't require transfer from rich countries.

## Where Redistribution Is Probably Required

### Social Determinants at Scale

TB illustrates the limit: you can cure a patient, discharge them, and they return to the malnutrition, overcrowding, and poor ventilation that caused infection in the first place. Cure rates are high; re-infection rates follow the structural conditions.

The social determinants of health — housing stability, nutrition, sanitation, environmental quality, stress from economic precarity — are causally upstream of disease burden in ways that neither drugs nor healthcare systems fully address. These require addressing structural poverty, which is redistribution by another name.

### Chronic Non-Communicable Disease

Infectious disease is the clearest case for technical solutions without redistribution. Chronic disease burden (cardiovascular disease, diabetes, cancer, mental health) is more tightly coupled to lifestyle and environment, which are in turn coupled to income and consumption patterns. Access to preventive care, quality food, and stress reduction — the inputs to NCD prevention — scale more directly with wealth than infectious disease treatment does.

## The Synthesis

A two-tier answer:

**Short-term, disease-specific**: Substantial progress on treatable infectious diseases is achievable without redistribution through IP reform, delivery infrastructure, and generic drug manufacturing. This is the strongest argument for targeted interventionism.

**Long-term, structural health**: Full health equity — "fair and just opportunity to be as healthy as possible" — requires addressing the structural conditions that create health vulnerabilities. Nutrition, housing, sanitation, environmental quality, and economic precarity are not solvable through healthcare systems alone. This is the honest limit of the non-redistributive case.

The Partners in Health framing: "the preferential option for the poor" means providing excellent healthcare to poor populations *while also* advocating for the structural changes that would make that healthcare less necessary. Both tracks are required.
