# Superforecasting

Philip Tetlock's research program — most fully presented in *Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction* (2015, with Dan Gardner) — on what separates consistently accurate forecasters from the general population, and what their habits reveal about learnable thinking skills.

## Background

Tetlock's original study (*Expert Political Judgment*, 2005) ran from 1984 to 2003 and accumulated nearly 28,000 predictions from 284 expert forecasters. The result was sobering: political and economic experts were barely more accurate than chance over long time horizons, and confident experts were often *less* accurate than cautious generalists. 

The follow-up Good Judgment Project (a US government intelligence initiative) recruited laypeople to make forecasting questions and found a small subset — "superforecasters" — who dramatically outperformed professional intelligence analysts.

## What Superforecasters Do Differently

The profile of the superforecaster maps closely onto the traits identified in [[latticework-pedagogy]] as prerequisites for latticework thinking:

| Superforecaster habit | Latticework equivalent |
|---|---|
| Active open-mindedness — actively seeking disconfirming evidence | Resisting confirmation bias and social proof |
| Aggregating across models — using multiple frameworks and averaging | Multi-model synthesis; "to a man with a hammer" problem |
| Treating beliefs as probability estimates, not commitments | Comfort with uncertainty; avoiding premature model closure |
| Frequent small updates on new evidence (vs. rare large opinion shifts) | Improvability of mental models |
| Distinguishing what is knowable from what is not | Circle of competence |

## Does Forecasting Practice Build Latticework Integration?

This is the key question from [[latticework-pedagogy]].

**The case that it does:**
- Superforecasting practice explicitly rewards multi-model aggregation: superforecasters who improve most do so by widening the range of models and base rates they consider before forming a view
- The practice of assigning and revising probabilities installs calibration as a disposition — the habit of *not* being more certain than the evidence warrants
- The mandatory feedback loop (forecasts are resolved; scores are calculated) provides exactly the low-latency feedback that [[disposition-vs-knowledge]] identifies as missing from most wisdom-building contexts
- The Good Judgment Project showed that even modest training interventions (a few hours) improved forecasting accuracy measurably — suggesting the cognitive habits are teachable

**The case that it doesn't fully transfer:**
- Superforecasting builds calibration and aggregation primarily for *near-term, resolvable geopolitical and economic events* — a specific domain
- [[latticework-pedagogy]] notes that "far transfer — applying a principle across distant domains — is rare and fragile without deep structural understanding of *why* the principle works." Forecasting practice does not guarantee that cross-domain integration
- Tetlock's superforecasters are drawn from a self-selected population of intellectually curious, motivated individuals — selection effects similar to those Munger's partnership self-selected for
- Calibration is not the same as wisdom: a superforecaster may know the probability that a policy will fail without having the deeper judgment about *why* it fails and what that implies for other domains

**Summary:** Forecasting practice reliably builds calibration, active open-mindedness, and the habit of aggregating across models — all components of the latticework disposition. But it builds these more reliably than it builds genuine far-transfer integration across distant domains. The superforecaster is a better version of a latticework practitioner, not a proof that the full latticework can be taught through forecasting alone.

## The Hedgehog vs. Fox Distinction

Tetlock's most cited finding (from *Expert Political Judgment*): forecasters who organize their worldview around *one big idea* (hedgehogs) are systematically less accurate than those who draw on *many different frameworks* (foxes). The fox/hedgehog distinction is effectively an empirical validation of Munger's latticework intuition — multi-model thinkers outperform single-lens thinkers on real-world predictions.

## Implications for [[disposition-building-practices]]

Structured forecasting is the most rigorous and evidence-backed of the three core disposition-building practices because:
1. It has the clearest feedback signal
2. The Good Judgment Project provides experimental (not just observational) evidence of learning
3. The specific mechanisms (probability calibration, active open-mindedness) are measurable and teachable

The recommended application: pair forecasting practice with decision journaling so that the *reasoning* behind probability estimates is recorded, not just the estimate. This captures the model-selection step that pure forecasting scores do not reward.
