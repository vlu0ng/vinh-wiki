# Focus vs. Orientation: The Tunnel Vision Problem

The failure mode of extreme focus strategies — including Keller's [[the-one-thing|ONE Thing]] — in which deep narrowing of attention prevents the peripheral awareness needed to notice whether you are pointed in the right direction.

## The Core Distinction

**Execution focus** (the ONE Thing, deep work, time-blocking) optimizes *within* a chosen direction. It answers: given that I'm building this thing, what's the most leveraged action right now?

**Orientation** is the prior question: am I building the right thing? Is this the right mountain? The ONE Thing model is excellent for execution and provides no tools for orientation.

The failure mode: applying execution focus *continuously*, never returning to orientation, and gradually losing the peripheral awareness that would reveal the direction is wrong.

## Four Specific Tunnel Vision Risks

### 1. Signal Filtering
Once committed to a focus, perceptual systems actively suppress competing signals — inattentional blindness and motivated cognition operating in concert. Important weak signals that don't fit the current "one thing" become literally invisible. The entrepreneur so focused on their product roadmap may miss early indicators of market shift that a more peripheral observer would catch immediately.

### 2. Adjacent Opportunity Cost
Large value-creation events often come from peripheral awareness: a different business's solution applicable to your domain, a relationship that opens an unexpected door, a method from an adjacent field. Deep focus makes these harder to notice. This is not an argument against focus; it's an argument for preserving dedicated time for peripheral scanning outside the execution sprint.

### 3. Failure to Update on Negative Feedback
Sunk cost effects are amplified in narrow-focus strategies. When you've structured your life and schedule around one thing, incoming evidence that it isn't working is harder to process honestly. The very commitment the ONE Thing creates (valuable for execution) becomes an obstacle to course correction. The framework provides no mechanism for recognizing when the focus itself should change.

### 4. Neglect Cascades
Keller acknowledges "counterbalancing" as a solution to neglected areas (relationships, health, peripheral competencies), but treats it as manageable through periodic rebalancing. Empirically, neglected areas frequently reach crisis points that retroactively undermine the very "one thing" they were sacrificed for. The executive neglecting relationships in pursuit of career outcomes who faces a divorce; the athlete neglecting recovery in pursuit of training volume who faces a career-ending injury.

## The Resolution: Separate Orientation from Execution

The practical integration:

1. **Quarterly/annual orientation phase**: broad peripheral scanning (reading widely, maintaining diverse weak ties, scheduled reflection on whether you're still climbing the right mountain). Deliberately *not* operating the ONE Thing logic. Ask: is this still the right direction? What am I not seeing?

2. **Execution sprints** (weeks to months): apply the ONE Thing / deep work / focus frameworks within the confirmed direction. Minimize peripheral distraction. Move the lead domino.

3. **Explicit stopping conditions for the orientation phase**: don't let orientation become [[experiment-theater|experiment theater]]. Define when "enough" scanning has happened and a direction is confirmed, then commit to execution.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

- [[focusing-question]]: the ONE Thing's core tool — excellent within an execution sprint, requires orientation context to be aimed correctly
- [[slow-productivity]]: Newport's "do fewer things" is the structural twin; both need a meta-strategy above them that asks whether the sprint is aimed correctly
- [[mental-models]]: Munger's approach is inherently more orientation-heavy — he reads widely, builds peripheral awareness, and waits for convergence before committing; the ONE Thing is more execution-oriented
- [[experiment-theater]]: the mirror failure — where experiment theater avoids commitment through perpetual exploration, tunnel vision avoids reorientation through perpetual execution

## The Deep Metaphor

Keller's domino image is apt for execution: the right first action enables progressively larger wins. But it assumes the dominoes are arranged correctly in the first place. Arrangement requires standing back, looking at the whole board, and sometimes resetting the sequence entirely. The framework is excellent for toppling the sequence you have; it doesn't help you know if you've arranged the right sequence.
