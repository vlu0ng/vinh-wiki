# Warren Buffett

Warren Buffett is the longtime chairman and CEO of Berkshire Hathaway and [[charlie-munger|Charlie Munger]]'s investing partner. In this wiki, Buffett mainly appears as the institutional and intellectual counterpart to Munger: Buffett supplied the value-investing base, while Munger helped push Berkshire toward buying excellent businesses at fair prices rather than merely cheap assets.

## Relevance to This Wiki

- Buffett anchors the Berkshire Hathaway context for [[mental-models-and-multidisciplinary-thinking]].
- His partnership with [[charlie-munger]] illustrates how durable decision-making cultures can be shaped by shared [[mental-models]].
- The Buffett-Munger pairing is a practical case of long-horizon judgment, patience, incentives, and circle-of-competence reasoning.

## Open Questions

- **Which Buffett writings or shareholder letters should be ingested to deepen the investing side of this wiki?**  
  The highest-priority primary sources: the 1977 and 2010 shareholder letters (where Buffett explains the business-quality shift most clearly), the "Owner's Manual" (1996, available on Berkshire's website, which defines Buffett's capital allocation principles), and *The Essays of Warren Buffett* (assembled by Lawrence Cunningham) which extracts and organizes themes across the letters. For the Munger intersection specifically, the 1995 letter (on See's Candies and the quality-vs-cheap distinction) and the letter discussing the American Express "salad oil scandal" (showing patience and conviction) are particularly relevant to this wiki's focus. The letters from 1977-1992 show the Graham → Munger transition most clearly. Adding these to `system/ingest-queue.md` as high priority would deepen the investing side significantly.
- **How much of Berkshire's philosophy is Buffett's original Graham-style value investing versus Munger's quality-business influence?**  
  The transition is traceable and both men have commented on it explicitly. The early Buffett (1957-1969, Buffett Partnership era) was pure Graham: statistical cheapness, "cigar butt" investing (buying deeply undervalued businesses for one last puff regardless of quality), high portfolio concentration, and strict price discipline. The Munger influence began in the 1960s and accelerated after See's Candies (1972) — the first major acquisition of a premium-priced quality business where Buffett paid well above book value. Munger's contribution: quality businesses with durable competitive advantages ("moats") deserve premium prices because their earnings compound for years; a cheap mediocre business is actually more expensive in terms of opportunity cost than a fairly-priced excellent one. Buffett's 1989 letter ("our preferred approach is to buy wonderful businesses at fair prices, not fair businesses at wonderful prices") marks the explicit articulation. Munger also pushed Buffett toward understanding psychological factors in business — management incentives, culture, customer loyalty — that Graham's balance-sheet focus underweighted.
