# Stéphane Breitwieser

(b. 1971, Mulhouse, France) — considered the **most prolific art thief in history**.

## Overview

Over roughly a decade spanning the 1990s and early 2000s, Breitwieser stole approximately **239 works** from museums, churches, and auction houses across Europe. Estimated combined value: hundreds of millions of dollars. He operated alongside his girlfriend **Anne-Catherine Kleinklaus**.

His defining characteristic: he **never sold a single stolen work**. Everything was kept in a secret two-room attic above his mother's house in Mulhouse, France.

## Method

- Posed as ordinary museum-visiting couple with Kleinklaus
- Scouted locations solo before each theft; memorized guard rotations and exit routes
- Exploited predictable lunchtime security gaps
- Used an oversized coat; Kleinklaus carried a large bag for concealment
- Preferred small, older works — ivory carvings, silver objects, enameled boxes — that could be concealed on the body

## First Major Theft

**February 1997, Rubens House, Antwerp** — an ivory sculpture of *Adam and Eve* by Georg Petel (1627). He had visited once before just to scout, became obsessed with the piece, and returned with Kleinklaus to steal it. The method: removing two screws from a plexiglass display box during the lunchtime guard gap.

## The Attic Collection

His private collection included:
- Ivory sculpture of Diana (Roman goddess of hunting)
- Ivory statuette of Catherine of Alexandria
- Ivory Cupid resting on a skull
- Napoleon's personal golden tobacco box in blue enamel
- Émile Gallé flower vase
- Silver goblet engraved with garlands
- Countless silver platters, bowls, pewter miniatures, gilded tea sets, medieval weapons

He arranged these on every available surface in his bedroom, described as a private museum more coherent than many public ones.

## Psychology

Breitwieser is a case study in [[compulsive-collecting]] decoupled entirely from monetary motivation. His stated drive: the desire to possess beauty, to touch what master craftsmen made centuries before, to wake up surrounded by transcendent objects.

The epigraph of Finkel's book: *"Aesthetics are higher than ethics." — Oscar Wilde*

## Arrest and Aftermath

His arrest and the subsequent fate of the collection form the second half of Finkel's narrative — including the destruction of works by his mother.
