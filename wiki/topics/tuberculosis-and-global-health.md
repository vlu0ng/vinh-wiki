# Tuberculosis and Global Health

A synthesis of John Green's *Everything Is Tuberculosis* (2025).

## What TB Is

Tuberculosis (TB) is an airborne bacterial infection caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. It spreads person-to-person through small particles in coughs, sneezes, and exhalations. Between one-quarter and one-third of all living humans carry the infection; up to 10% will eventually develop active disease.

Classic symptoms: persistent cough, night sweats, fever, and physical wasting — historically called "consumption."

## Scale of the Problem

- Over **1 billion** people have died of TB in the last two centuries — estimated 1 in 7 of all humans who have ever lived
- **1,250,000** people died of TB in 2023 — more than malaria, typhoid, and war combined
- TB was the world's deadliest infectious disease for most of recorded history; COVID-19 displaced it 2020–2022, but TB reclaimed that title in 2023
- TB has been **curable since the mid-1950s**

## The Central Paradox: A Curable Disease That Kills Millions

Green's core argument: TB's persistence is a moral and political failure, not a scientific one. We have known how to cure TB for 70 years. The problem is distribution.

Ugandan physician Dr. Peter Mugyenyi, speaking about AIDS in 2000: *"The drugs are where the disease is not. And the disease is where the drugs are not."*

Green argues the same is true of TB today.

## How We Imagine Illness Shapes Who Lives and Dies

Historical framings of TB, each influencing who received care:
- A mechanical failure of the lungs (James Watt's era)
- An inherited disease affecting certain "types" of personalities
- Caused by demon possession, poisoned air, God's judgment, or whiskey
- A disease of the poor and the racially marginalized

Green's thesis: **how we understand illness is never neutral** — it determines social priorities, resource allocation, and ultimately, who dies.

### The Romantic Disease Myth

For roughly two centuries (c. 1750–1950), TB was romanticized as the "artist's disease" — a mark of creative genius and spiritual refinement. The wasting, pallor, feverish intensity, and early death were aestheticized rather than medicalized. Keats, Chopin, Kafka, Modigliani, Chekhov, Orwell — so many prominent artists died of TB that the association became self-reinforcing.

This myth caused direct harm: it concentrated sympathy (and sanatorium care) on middle-class consumptives while the poor — who bore the highest disease burden — were instead blamed for moral failing. The romanticization of TB among artists helped justify neglect of TB among the masses.

See [[romanticization-of-illness]] for the full concept, including Susan Sontag's *Illness as Metaphor* analysis.

## Drug-Resistant TB

The book includes chapters on "Superbug" and "Vicious Cycles," addressing drug-resistant TB strains that develop when treatment is incomplete or inconsistent — a direct consequence of inadequate access to full drug courses.

## Open Questions

- What would full TB eradication require politically and financially?
- **How does drug-resistant TB change the cure timeline?**  
  Drug-susceptible TB requires 6 months of standard first-line therapy (HRZE: isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol). Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB, resistant to isoniazid and rifampicin) historically required 18-24 months of second-line drugs (injectables, fluoroquinolones) that are more toxic and cost roughly 100× as much. Extensively drug-resistant TB (XDR-TB, additionally resistant to fluoroquinolones and second-line injectables) was historically nearly untreatable. Recent advances have improved this: the BPaL regimen (bedaquiline, pretomanid, linezolid) now offers a 6-month treatment for XDR-TB with success rates around 90% in clinical trials — a genuine breakthrough. But BPaL is expensive and access is limited by the same IP and distribution barriers that limit first-line drugs. The cure timeline for MDR/XDR-TB in high-burden countries (India, China, South Africa, Russia, Philippines) is still substantially extended because the newer regimens are not yet broadly accessible, and patient adherence over 6+ months in low-resource settings remains a challenge. Drug resistance is itself a downstream consequence of inadequate access to complete first-line courses — making it a self-amplifying problem.
- **What can TB's history teach us about COVID-19, HIV, and future pandemics?**  
  Several lessons emerge with enough specificity to be actionable: (1) *Cures don't cure inequality* — TB became curable in the 1950s; seventy years later it still kills 1.25 million per year. The pattern repeated exactly with HIV (effective ART available by 1996; millions died in Africa through the 2000s because drugs were where disease was not) and with COVID vaccines (mRNA vaccines developed in under a year; low-income countries reached coverage years later). The lesson is not that we need faster science but that distribution is the predictable crisis, and we consistently fail to plan for it in advance. (2) *Romanticization and stigma are both lethal, by the same mechanism* — TB was sentimentalized for the middle class and blamed on the poor. HIV was blamed on gay men and drug users, delaying funding and political will for years. COVID produced the same pattern (it only kills the old; it only kills the comorbid). When illness can be narrated as something that happens to *them*, political will for collective response collapses. (3) *Partial treatment breeds resistance* — incomplete TB courses created MDR-TB and XDR-TB, requiring 18–24 month regimens costing 100× as much as drug-susceptible TB. Incomplete HIV treatment creates resistant strains. Large unvaccinated COVID populations created variant emergence conditions. Cheap early intervention is always cheaper than expensive late intervention, but health systems consistently underfund the former. (4) *The political economy of neglect is stable* — TB spent decades as a "solved problem" in wealthy countries while killing millions annually in poor ones. Funding follows disease burden in rich countries, not global burden. This will be the pattern for the next pandemic unless governance (WHO funding, pandemic treaty) is deliberately redesigned. (5) *Community trust is the rate-limiting factor for all interventions* — germ theory required cultural adoption; directly observed TB therapy (DOT) succeeded where health worker relationships were strong and failed where they were absent; COVID vaccine rollout succeeded fastest where institutional trust was highest. Building trusted community health workers and functional primary care is a pandemic-preparedness investment, not just a development goal.
