# The ONE Thing

## Overview / Core Thesis

Gary Keller and Jay Papasan argue that extraordinary results come from narrowing focus to a single most-important
action — what they call "going small." The central tool is the **Focusing Question**: *"What's the ONE thing I can do
such that by doing it, everything else will be easier or unnecessary?"*

The book's opening image comes from the film *City Slickers*: cowboy Curly holds up one finger and says "One thing.
Just one thing." Mitch asks what the one thing is. Curly: "That's what you've got to figure out." That's the book.

## Going Small

"Going small" is counterintuitive. Most people think big success requires doing more. Keller argues the opposite:
extraordinary results are directly determined by how narrow you can make your focus.

- Spread across too many priorities → everything suffers
- One clear priority → domino effect — each success topples the next
- Success is *sequential*, not *simultaneous*

## The Domino Effect

A small domino can knock over a domino 50% larger. This geometric progression means that the right first action,
done well, enables progressively larger wins. Keller's metaphor for career and project planning: line up the dominoes
(sequence your priorities correctly) and tip the first one.

## Six Lies That Mislead

The book systematically dismantles common productivity myths:
1. Everything matters equally (vs. prioritization by leverage)
2. Multitasking is productive (vs. task-switching costs)
3. A disciplined life is required (vs. selective discipline on the one thing)
4. Willpower is on-call (vs. willpower as a depletable resource)
5. Balance is achievable (vs. intentional counterbalancing over time)
6. Big is bad (vs. thinking big as prerequisite for big outcomes)

## The Focusing Question

Used at every scale:
- **Life level**: What's the ONE thing I want my life to be about?
- **Annual level**: What's the ONE thing I can do this year to make progress toward that goal?
- **Weekly/daily**: What's the ONE thing I can do today such that by doing it, everything else becomes easier or unnecessary?

The question forces prioritization by elimination. Keller describes coaches shortening their recap lists from five
items to three to two — then finally to one — and watching results improve dramatically with each reduction.

## Open Questions

- **The ONE Thing model implicitly assumes autonomy over your schedule. How does it apply to reactive knowledge-worker roles with constant interruptions?**  
  The framework's leverage point is *protecting a block of time* for the one thing before reactivity begins — not restructuring the entire day. Newport's time-block planning research and Gloria Mark's interruption work both support this: even 60-90 uninterrupted minutes in the morning (before the reactive workday starts) are sufficient to make meaningful progress on the high-leverage task. The prescriptive answer: apply the ONE Thing at the *daily planning* level by front-loading it before reactive obligations, not by eliminating reactivity. For roles with severe interruption constraints (ER medicine, elementary teaching, customer support), the planning horizon shifts: the ONE Thing operates at the weekly or project level, with execution opportunistically distributed across available windows. The model's weakest fit is truly reactive roles where no autonomous time is structurally available and task-switching is literally the job description — which is genuinely under-theorized in Keller's framework.
- **At what point does "going small" become tunnel vision that misses important signals outside your one thing?**  
  The failure mode is predictable: the ONE Thing model is excellent for *execution phases* and poor for *orientation phases*. Keller's framework optimizes within a chosen domain — given you're building a real estate business, what's the one thing? — but does not help you notice when the domain itself is wrong. Tunnel risks include: (1) *Signal filtering* — once committed to a focus, our perceptual systems actively suppress competing signals (inattentional blindness, motivated cognition); important weak signals that don't fit the current "one thing" become invisible; (2) *Adjacent opportunity cost* — large value-creation events often come from peripheral awareness: a different business's solution that applies to your domain, a relationship that opens an unexpected door; deep focus makes these harder to catch; (3) *Failure to update on negative feedback* — sunk cost effects are more powerful in narrow-focus strategies; you've structured your life around this one thing, so incoming evidence it's not working is harder to process honestly; (4) *Neglect cascades* — Keller acknowledges counterbalancing but treats it as manageable; empirically, neglected areas (relationships, health, peripheral competencies) frequently reach crisis points that retroactively undermine the very "one thing" they were sacrificed for. The practical resolution is to separate *orientation* from *execution*: use broad peripheral scanning (reading widely, maintaining diverse weak ties, scheduled reflection on whether you're still climbing the right mountain) for quarterly/annual orientation, then apply the ONE Thing framework within execution sprints. This is compatible with Cal Newport's deep work — both work within a sprint but need a meta-strategy above them that asks whether the sprint itself is aimed correctly.
