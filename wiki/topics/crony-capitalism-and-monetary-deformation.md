# Crony Capitalism and Monetary Deformation

## Overview / Core Thesis

[[david-stockman|David Stockman]]'s *The Great Deformation* (2013) argues that American capitalism has been systematically corrupted by a [[crony-capitalism|crony capitalist]] alliance between Wall Street and Washington. The Federal Reserve's abandonment of [[sound-money|sound money]] in favor of [[bubble-finance|bubble finance]], combined with bipartisan bailout culture, has created a permanent fiscal cliff that neither party has the will or ability to address. The deformation is not a partisan failure but a structural one rooted in decades of monetary and fiscal decisions going back to Nixon's 1971 closing of the gold window.

## Key Ideas

### The 2008 Blackberry Panic
Stockman contends the TARP bailout was unnecessary and driven by panic rather than systemic necessity. His claim: no "dark ATMs" or imminent banking collapse — just a hissy fit on Wall Street that Hank Paulson (a Goldman Sachs transplant) used to extract massive public subsidies for Wall Street firms, AIG, and eventually automakers. The "contagion" narrative was a cover story.

### The Reagan Legacy Reexamined
Stockman served as Reagan's budget director and argues the "Reagan Revolution" was largely mythologized. Reagan's welfare state was barely 0.5% of GDP smaller than Carter's; defense spending ballooned; structural deficits became permanent. The supply-side revolution gave way to crony tax-cutting for K Street lobbies, not principled fiscal conservatism.

### Nixon's Abomination — August 1971
The closure of the gold window and the shift to floating fiat currencies is Stockman's original sin. It untethered monetary policy from discipline and enabled the Federal Reserve to become an engine of inflation, bubble finance, and political interference in markets.

### The Age of Bubble Finance
From Greenspan through Bernanke, the Fed's response to every downturn was to reflate asset prices rather than allow market clearing. This created serial bubbles (tech, housing, LBOs) and rewarded financial engineering over productive investment. Wall Street became enormous relative to the real economy.

### Sundown in America
Stockman's prognosis is dark: with deficits as far as the eye can see, a captured central bank, entrenched lobbies, and a political system unable to make hard choices, free markets and representative democracy are in long-term decline.

## Open Questions
- Is Stockman's prescription (sound money, no bailouts, fiscal austerity) politically achievable or merely principled?
- **Does his libertarian lens cause him to underweight the systemic risks he dismisses (e.g., was 2008 actually catastrophic without intervention)?**  
  Yes, substantially. Stockman's "Blackberry Panic" framing — that TARP was unnecessary theater by Goldman alumni — does not hold up against the empirical record. In September 2008, commercial paper markets froze, money market funds "broke the buck" after Lehman (triggering a run that nearly destroyed the $3.5T money-market industry), and interbank lending essentially stopped. These are measurable seizures in the core plumbing of the financial system, not just Wall Street melodrama. Bernanke, a serious scholar of the Great Depression, was specifically responding to the mechanism by which 1929 became the 1930s: bank-run contagion propagating through the real economy via credit contraction. Counterfactual analysis (Gorton, Acemoglu and others) suggests that without intervention, cascading failures could have pushed unemployment well above the ~10% actual peak — potentially toward 1930s levels. Stockman's libertarian priors lead him to read moral hazard (which is real) as proof that the underlying risk was manufactured, when in fact both can be true simultaneously. Where Stockman is *correct*: the bailout terms were too favorable to shareholders and executives (Goldman received 100 cents on the dollar on AIG contracts); the moral hazard created by "too big to fail" was severe and left unaddressed; subsequent QE programs went beyond crisis-fighting into asset-price management that concentrated gains to capital. The correct synthesis is that intervention of *some kind* was probably necessary to prevent a depression, while the specific intervention that occurred was poorly structured and captured by the interests it was meant to discipline.
- How has the post-2013 decade of QE, COVID stimulus, and crypto validated or invalidated his thesis?
