# Economic Literacy and the Vibes Economy

## Overview / Core Thesis

Kyla Scanlon's *In This Economy?* argues that economic illiteracy persists not because economics is intrinsically
complex, but because it is taught as pure math/logic while ignoring the fact that economies are composed of emotional,
irrational humans. The gap between economic theory and lived reality is the central problem — and storytelling,
analogy, and vibes-awareness are the tools to close it.

The foreword by Morgan Housel: despite massive gains in engineering and medicine over a century, we show no clear
progress in economics or personal finance. Why? Because economic outcomes depend on human behavior, which doesn't
obey iron laws.

## Core Framework: The Economy as People

The most fundamental reframe: *"The economy is really a bunch of people peopling around."* GDP, monetary policy,
inflation — these are not mechanical forces; they are aggregate expressions of billions of individual decisions shaped
by fear, hope, information asymmetry, and social norms.

This means:
- Economic events are partly self-fulfilling (expectations matter as much as fundamentals)
- Fedspeak (deliberately vague central bank communication) is not incidental — it's a feature to prevent market
  overreaction
- Most economic coverage optimizes for engagement over understanding, producing alarmism

## Vibecession

Scanlon coined the term **"vibecession"** in 2022 to describe a situation where economic indicators are technically
healthy but people *feel* like they're in a recession — and that feeling can itself become a self-fulfilling prophecy.

It captures the gap between:
- **Hard data**: unemployment low, GDP growing
- **Soft data / vibes**: consumer sentiment depressed, social media pessimism, persistent anxiety about cost of living

The vibecession concept illustrates that behavioral economics and psychology belong at the center of economic analysis,
not as footnotes.

## Why Economics Education Fails

- Taught as math with "right answers" — ignores the behavioral gap
- Uses jargon to signal expertise rather than communicate (Fedspeak, yield curve, secular stagnation)
- Expert forecasting record is poor: *The Economist*'s January previews for 2020, 2022, and 2023 missed COVID,
  Ukraine, and Hamas attacks respectively — not as failures but as reminders of fundamental uncertainty

## Key Structures Covered

- **Business cycles**: economies trend upward toward growth but cycle through expansion/contraction
- **Federal Reserve mechanics**: how interest rates, money supply, and inflation interact
- **Behavioral pushes and pulls**: how anchoring, loss aversion, and social proof drive economic decisions
- **Personal finance implications**: why knowing the macro matters for individual decisions

## Open Questions

- **How do you build economic literacy at scale without the content being captured by political narratives?**  
  The core strategy: anchor literacy in *mechanics* (how does the Fed set interest rates?) rather than *evaluation* (should rates be lower?). Mechanistic questions have empirically verifiable answers; evaluative questions embed normative choices that map onto political positions. Behavioral economics provides relatively non-partisan ground — cognitive biases are empirically documented and no political coalition "owns" the critique. The vibecession concept itself is an example: an observation about the data-sentiment gap that doesn't prescribe a political response. But Dan Kahan's "motivated numeracy" research warns that higher statistical literacy doesn't reduce political polarization on contested questions — it increases it, because people use analytical skills to defend prior beliefs. The deeper solution may be identity-decoupled pedagogy: grounding concepts in personal narrative and shared experience so they're less immediately retrievable as tribal weapons. See [[economic-literacy-at-scale]] for the full analysis.
- **Scanlon's framing is optimistic (economies trend toward okay-ness). How does that hold up against structural inequality and climate risk?**  
  The optimism is well-calibrated within its domain (normal business-cycle dynamics, aggregate, developed economies) but faces two structural challenges. First, distributional inequality: the K-shaped COVID recovery showed that aggregate indicators can look healthy while large segments trend downward; Piketty's r > g argument suggests inequality compounds structurally even in growing economies; some vibecession sentiment may be rational rather than miscalibrated — reflecting people accurately reading their own K-shaped economy. Second, climate risk introduces secular headwinds with no cyclical recovery mechanism: physical climate risk (agricultural disruption, mass displacement), transition risk (stranded assets, energy cost volatility), and long-horizon habitability questions are outside the domain where business-cycle optimism applies. Scanlon's corrective against doom-optimizing financial media remains valid; the scope condition is more limited than her framing implies. See [[scanlon-optimism-limits]] for the full critique.
